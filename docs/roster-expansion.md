Last updated: 2026-04-16

Research was conducted using the Critical Decision Method pipeline requirements (`framework_forge/`) as a grounding constraint — each recommendation is filtered against the pipeline's need for 20–30 critical incidents with *visible reasoning*, not just outcomes.

## Part 2: Ranked Shortlist — Extract in This Order

### #1 — Marcus Aurelius

**Hook:** The most powerful man in the world spent 20 years writing a private journal about how not to abuse power. He never published it. That energy — Stoicism applied to real stakes — is what millions of people are already searching for via Ryan Holiday, Tim Ferriss, and the broader Stoicism renaissance.

**Best for decisions about:** Leading without ego, maintaining composure under crisis, resisting the temptation of short-term wins, staying principle-anchored when you have the power to cut corners.

**Source material:** *Meditations* is the pipeline's dream input — it is literally 12 books of private CDM reasoning. Every entry is him noticing a cue, framing a situation, and deciding how to respond. Add Cassius Dio, Anthony Birley's biography, and Pierre Hadot's *The Inner Citadel*. 25+ extractable incidents with visible reasoning is trivial.

**Watch-outs:** Slight overlap with Sun Tzu on "military philosophy" framing, but Aurelius is fundamentally a civilian/ethical thinker. His "decisions" are often internal — could require careful framing in the pipeline to map to external critical incidents. Not a weakness, just a prompt-craft consideration.

---

### #2 — Abraham Lincoln

**Hook:** The man who held a country together during its worst crisis by making decisions that made everyone angry — and was right. "Think like Lincoln" resonates for anyone navigating a situation where every option has a terrible cost.

**Best for decisions about:** Crisis leadership, coalition management, timing (when to act vs. wait), communicating under fire, holding competing values simultaneously without collapsing into false resolution.

**Source material:** Exceptional. Roy Basler's 9-volume *Collected Works of Abraham Lincoln* is public domain. Doris Kearns Goodwin's *Team of Rivals*, the Lincoln-Douglas debates, the Emancipation Proclamation deliberations (multiple drafts with his handwritten reasoning), and thousands of letters to generals. More documented decision reasoning than almost any figure on this list.

**Watch-outs:** A few decisions (Emancipation timing, McClellan firing) get over-studied. The pipeline should spread across his law career, congressional years, and wartime rather than only the famous moments.

---

### #3 — Benjamin Franklin

**Hook:** The original polymath who actually shipped. Invented bifocals, organized the first fire department, negotiated the treaty that won the Revolution, and wrote the self-improvement playbook that still sells. "Think like Franklin" = practical genius.

**Best for decisions about:** Entrepreneurship and founding-stage decisions, negotiation, scientific experimentation as a decision model, self-improvement systems, diplomacy.

**Source material:** Rich. *Autobiography* is public domain. *Poor Richard's Almanack* shows applied heuristics. Yale's Franklin Papers project has 47 volumes of letters. He was compulsively self-documenting — he literally kept a virtue tracking notebook. Pipeline-ready.

**Watch-outs:** Some decisions are morally complicated (he was an enslaver for years before becoming an abolitionist). His actual moral evolution is itself a decision-making case study — should be handled honestly, not sanitized.

---

### #4 — Seneca

**Hook:** The Stoic philosopher who was also a billionaire, Nero's advisor, and eventually ordered to kill himself — and wrote his most lucid work in the days before his death. His decision-making is tested in the ultimate pressure cooker.

**Best for decisions about:** Navigating corrupt or impossible institutions, decisions about when to stay vs. leave, managing the gap between stated values and daily behavior, mortality and long-term thinking.

**Source material:** *Letters to Lucilius* (124 letters explicitly reasoning through decisions), *On the Shortness of Life*, *On Benefits*, *Natural Questions*. All public domain with multiple translations. Extremely high reasoning-visibility — he wrote *about* how he decided, not just what he decided.

**Watch-outs:** Overlap with Marcus Aurelius in the Stoicism frame. Differentiation: Seneca is about practical ethics under institutional compromise; Aurelius is about inner self-command. Keep that distinction clear in the product.

---

### #5 — Napoleon Bonaparte

**Hook:** The most studied military and organizational decision-maker in history. Every business strategy course references him. "What would Napoleon do?" has been asked by generals, executives, and chess players for 200 years.

**Best for decisions about:** Competitive strategy, speed vs. deliberation tradeoffs, organizing complex operations with incomplete information, knowing when to press and when to consolidate.

**Source material:** Excellent. 60,000+ letters in the French national archives (partial translations available). *Maxims of War* and *Saint Helena Memoirs*. Andrew Roberts's *Napoleon: A Life* (2014) is the modern scholarly standard. The Waterloo campaign alone has 25+ documented decision points with visible reasoning.

**Watch-outs:** Ethical record is genuinely mixed (reinstated slavery in Haiti, 3+ million battlefield deaths). The product should not lionize uncritically — the CDM framework captures his methodology, not a moral endorsement.

---

### #6 — Frederick Douglass

**Hook:** Escaped slavery, taught himself to read in secret, became the most powerful orator of the 19th century, and strategically navigated abolitionism, the Civil War, and Reconstruction. He made every major decision with his life on the line.

**Best for decisions about:** Persuasion and reframing, navigating systems designed to exclude you, when to compromise vs. hold the line, building moral authority through consistency, rhetoric as a decision tool.

**Source material:** Exceptional and underused. Three autobiographies (*Narrative of the Life*, *My Bondage and My Freedom*, *Life and Times*) — all public domain, all showing his reasoning evolution. Thousands of speeches. The Library of Congress holds his papers digitized. Highly pipeline-ready.

**Watch-outs:** Less overlap with existing roster than almost any candidate — strong diversity signal, unique decision domain (moral-strategic). No legal concerns. Arguably underrepresented in the "great decision-maker" canon, which is itself a differentiator for the product.

---

### #7 — Florence Nightingale

**Hook:** She didn't just nurse soldiers — she redesigned the entire British military hospital system by showing commanders, in their own language (data), that they were killing more men than the enemy was. She invented the pie chart. "Think like Nightingale" = systems change through evidence.

**Best for decisions about:** Making the case for change inside resistant institutions, data-driven decision-making, prioritization under resource constraints, translating expertise into influence.

**Source material:** Strong. She wrote 14,000+ letters (Florence Nightingale Museum holds digitized copies). *Notes on Nursing* and her statistical reports to Parliament are public domain. The Crimea War correspondence shows real-time decision-making under chaos. Lynn McDonald's 16-volume collected works is the modern scholarly edition.

**Watch-outs:** Less name recognition in "decision-making" contexts than in "nursing pioneer" ones. Product copy needs to surface the strategic/systems thinker angle. The pipeline will handle this correctly if it focuses on her data and institutional influence decisions.

---

### #8 — Harriet Tubman

**Hook:** Nineteen missions into slave territory, zero losses. She operated under conditions where a single mistake was death — and made decisions that consistently beat the odds. "Think like Tubman" = radical risk calibration and operational trust.

**Best for decisions about:** High-stakes irreversible decisions, trusting your read of a situation over institutional authority, mission commitment vs. abort criteria, leading people who are terrified.

**Source material:** Moderate but sufficient. Kate Clifford Larson's *Bound for the Promised Land* and Kate Bradford's 1869 *Scenes in the Life* are the primary reconstructions. Contemporary abolitionist accounts (Thomas Garrett, William Still's *The Underground Railroad*) provide corroborating incident records. 20+ extractable incidents is achievable, though it requires more synthesis than Franklin or Lincoln.

**Watch-outs:** No primary sources in her own hand — she was kept illiterate by law. All reasoning is inferred from secondhand accounts and interviews. The pipeline needs to be carefully grounded in well-sourced biographies rather than popular retellings.

---

### #9 — Julius Caesar

**Hook:** He crossed the Rubicon, wrote his own account of why, and changed the world. His *Gallic Wars* is the rare case of a world-historical figure narrating his own decision-making in real time.

**Best for decisions about:** Decisive action at the point of no return, military/competitive strategy, political maneuvering, building personal loyalty as a strategic asset.

**Source material:** Good. *Commentarii de Bello Gallico* and *de Bello Civili* are first-person accounts — unusual richness for ancient history. Plutarch and Suetonius add behavioral observations. Adrian Goldsworthy's modern biography is excellent. 25 incidents is achievable.

**Watch-outs:** Overlaps with Sun Tzu (military strategy) and Alexander stub (already committed) in domain. Caesar's differentiation is political + military integration, and his first-person accounts. Frame him as the "power + politics" figure vs. Sun Tzu's pure strategy. Extract after the Alexander stub resolves.

---

### #10 — Andrew Carnegie

**Hook:** The son of a handloom weaver who became the richest man in the world, then gave it all away. His decisions about when to bet, when to consolidate, and what money is actually *for* make him a business and philanthropy decision model.

**Best for decisions about:** Capital allocation, competitive focus, long-term wealth-building strategy, the pivot from accumulation to giving, legacy and impact decisions.

**Source material:** Good. *The Gospel of Wealth* essay, *Autobiography of Andrew Carnegie* (public domain), Carnegie Corporation archives, Joseph Frazier Wall's 1970 biography is the definitive scholarly source. Business decisions at Carnegie Steel are extensively documented in company records and congressional testimony.

**Watch-outs:** The Homestead Strike (1892) is a serious ethical stain — he crushed a union while preaching worker dignity. Same note as Napoleon: don't sanitize. The strike itself is an excellent critical incident — the gap between his stated values and his actual decision is exactly what the CDM framework should capture.

## Part 3: Existing Roster — Legal Flags

### Einstein — Hidden pending legal review

Einstein's framework has been extracted and is complete, but his entry has been hidden from the product UI (`minds.ts` in company-builder, `frameworks.ts` in website) pending a legal check.

**Situation:** Hebrew University of Jerusalem (HUJ) holds registered trademarks on "Albert Einstein" for commercial categories and earns ~$12.5M/year in licensing. A 2012 California federal court (General Motors LLC v. Hebrew University) ruled that Einstein's *right of publicity* expired under New Jersey law ~2005, and HUJ lost that enforcement action. However, HUJ still holds active trademark registrations and continues to send cease-and-desist letters as a tactic.

**Risk profile:** Using his name and documented ideas in a text-based product is defensible based on the court ruling. Using his photograph or stylized portrait in product imagery is where HUJ actively pursues claims. An AI "think like Einstein" product sits in a gray zone that warrants a brief legal consult before scaling marketing on his name.

**To re-enable:** Remove `hidden: true` comment markers in:
- `company-builder/src/data/minds.ts` (the Einstein entry block)
- `website/src/lib/frameworks.ts` (restore `albert-einstein` to `ALLOWED_SLUGS`, `SLUG_COLOR_VAR`, `ERA_FALLBACK`, `DISPLAY_ORDER`)

### Tesla — Clean

Died 1943 (83 years ago). No active estate enforcement in the US. No enforceable right of publicity. Full public domain for writings. The Tesla Motors trademark is irrelevant — it covers electric vehicles, not a historical figure product. No action needed.

### All others (Newton, da Vinci, Curie, Machiavelli, Sun Tzu) — Safe

All died 400+ years ago. Zero IP risk.

## Part 4: Pipeline Requirements Summary

Based on `framework_forge/` (`framework_forge/config.py`, `framework_forge/cli.py`, stage scripts):

**Minimum to run an extraction:**
- 8–15 curated source text files (`.txt`) placed in `frameworks/{slug}/sources/`
- Source priority: critical incidents under pressure > value conflicts > failures > private writings > published works > biographies > secondary analysis
- Must yield **20–30 critical incidents** with visible reasoning (the CDM probes need: cues noticed, goals, rejected alternatives, situation framing, expected outcome)
- Domain string + full name to run `python -m framework_forge.cli discover_sources`

**This disqualifies or downgrades:**
- **Cleopatra** (stub): Sourcing is almost entirely hostile secondhand. Hard to hit 20 strong CDM incidents without speculation. Reconsider stub priority.
- **Genghis Khan**: *Secret History of the Mongols* is filtered through court scribes. Incident richness is lower than it looks.
- **Joan of Arc**: Trial transcripts are surprisingly rich for CDM (her interrogators were forcing her to explain her reasoning), but needs expert framing. Viable but challenging.
- **Socrates**: All via Plato — philosophically rich but reasoning is Plato's reconstruction. Works for a "reasoning methodology" frame but is philosophically contested.
