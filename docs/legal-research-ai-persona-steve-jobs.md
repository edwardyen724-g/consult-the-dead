## 1. Right of Publicity / Personality Rights

### California Post-Mortem Right of Publicity (Civil Code 3344.1)

California's post-mortem right of publicity protects a deceased personality's "name, voice, signature, photograph, or likeness" for **70 years after death**. Steve Jobs died in 2011, meaning these rights remain active until approximately **2081**.

**AB 1836 Amendment (effective January 1, 2025):** California expanded Section 3344.1 to cover AI "digital replicas." However, the statutory definition is narrowly focused:

> A "digital replica" is defined as "a computer-generated, highly realistic electronic representation that is readily identifiable as the **voice or visual likeness** of an individual that is embodied in a **sound recording, image, audiovisual work, or transmission**."

**Critical Finding:** AB 1836's definition is tied to voice and visual likeness in audio/visual media -- NOT text-based representations of someone's reasoning style or personality. A text-based reasoning framework that applies Jobs' documented thinking patterns without replicating his voice or visual appearance would likely **fall outside** the scope of this specific statute.

**However:** The broader California right-of-publicity statute (Civil Code 3344 and 3344.1, pre-AB 1836) protects a deceased personality's **name** in addition to voice/likeness. Using Jobs' name directly (e.g., "Think Like Steve Jobs") could implicate these protections even without voice/visual replication.

**Penalties under AB 1836:** $10,000 minimum or actual damages, whichever is greater.

**Exceptions (First Amendment):**
- News, sports, and public affairs
- Comment, criticism, satire, parody, or scholarship
- Documentary use, biography, historical works (with "some degree of fictionalization" permitted, unless it creates a false impression that the replica is the actual personality)

### The Key Legal Question: "Impersonation" vs. "Analysis"

The legal distinction between these two approaches is not yet well-established in case law, but the emerging standard is a **public perception test**: if the AI output is recognizable as a specific celebrity to a reasonable person, it likely triggers right-of-publicity claims regardless of labeling.

| Approach | Risk Level | Reasoning |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| "I am Steve Jobs, ask me anything" | **HIGH** | Direct impersonation using name + persona |
| "Chat with Steve Jobs' AI" | **HIGH** | Uses name, implies endorsed persona |
| "This tool thinks like Steve Jobs" | **MEDIUM-HIGH** | Uses name, implies replication of personality |
| "Framework inspired by Jobs' documented principles" | **MEDIUM-LOW** | Attribution without impersonation |
| "First-principles product thinking framework" (no name) | **LOW** | No name, no likeness, abstract methodology |

## 2. Recent AI Persona Legal Cases

### George Carlin Estate v. Dudesy (2024) -- SETTLED

The Carlin estate sued over an AI-generated comedy special. The creators agreed to:
- Permanently delete the special
- A permanent injunction barring use of Carlin's image, voice, or likeness on any platform

The estate's attorney called it "a blueprint for resolving similar disputes going forward." Key fact: even though the script was allegedly written by a human (not AI), the use of Carlin's voice/persona was enough to trigger the claim.

**Relevance to our project:** This case involved voice replication and direct impersonation. A text-based reasoning framework is substantially different, but the case signals aggressive enforcement by estates.

### Scarlett Johansson / OpenAI (2024) -- No Formal Lawsuit Filed

OpenAI created a voice ("Sky") that sounded like Johansson after she declined to license her voice. Key precedent cited: **Midler v. Ford (1988)** -- the Ninth Circuit found that using a sound-alike voice after the celebrity declined was actionable under California right of publicity.

OpenAI withdrew the voice. No formal lawsuit was filed, but the incident shaped legislative action.

### Character.AI -- Lawsuits (2024-2025)

Character.AI faces lawsuits primarily around **child safety**, not celebrity impersonation. A federal judge ruled in May 2025 that AI chatbots are "products" subject to product liability, not protected speech under the First Amendment. No major celebrity-impersonation lawsuit has been filed against Character.AI to date, despite the platform hosting many celebrity-styled chatbots.

### SAG-AFTRA v. Llama Productions (2025)

Filed an unfair labor practice charge over AI-generated James Earl Jones voice for Darth Vader in Fortnite after Jones' death, arguing unauthorized replication of a deceased performer's voice.

### Federal Legislative Landscape

The **NO FAKES Act** was reintroduced in April 2025 with bipartisan support and backing from SAG-AFTRA, OpenAI, Disney, Google, and 400+ artists including Scarlett Johansson. As of early 2026, it has **NOT been passed into law**. It would create a federal right for both living and deceased individuals regarding "digital replicas" -- defined similarly to AB 1836 (voice and visual likeness focus).

## 3. Copyright Analysis

### Is a "Thinking Style" Copyrightable?

**No.** Under 17 U.S.C. Section 102(b), copyright does not protect "any idea, procedure, process, system, method of operation, concept, principle, or discovery." A person's thinking style, decision-making framework, or reasoning methodology is a **method or system** and is categorically uncopyrightable.

**What IS protected:**
- The specific **written expression** describing the framework (e.g., Isaacson's biography text, specific interview transcripts)
- Exact **quotations** from Jobs' speeches, interviews, or writings
- The **selection and arrangement** of material in a biography or documentary

**What is NOT protected:**
- The underlying ideas, principles, or methods (e.g., "focus on simplicity," "say no to 1000 things," "intersection of technology and liberal arts")
- General thinking patterns or decision-making approaches
- Publicly known facts about Jobs' management style

### Fair Use Considerations

If the framework references or analyzes copyrighted source material:

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| Purpose and character of use | Transformative analysis favors fair use |
| Nature of copyrighted work | Published biographical/factual works -- neutral |
| Amount used | Using short quotes vs. long excerpts matters significantly |
| Market effect | A reasoning framework does not substitute for buying the biography |

**Risk mitigation:** Extracting *principles* from source material (fair use likely applies) is very different from reproducing *lengthy passages* from Isaacson's biography (likely infringement).

## 4. Safe Approaches and Risk Mitigation

### Framing: Framework Analysis vs. Impersonation

The safest approach is to frame the tool as an **analytical framework derived from publicly documented principles** rather than a simulation of the person:

**SAFER: "This framework applies product thinking principles documented in published interviews, biographies, and public speeches by technology leaders including Steve Jobs."**

**RISKIER: "Talk to Steve Jobs AI" or "What would Steve Jobs say?"**

### Recommended Disclaimers

1. "This tool is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or authorized by Apple Inc., the Steve Jobs estate, or any related party."
2. "This framework is based on publicly documented principles and published analysis. It does not claim to represent, simulate, or replicate any individual's actual thinking."
3. "No AI-generated output should be attributed to any real person, living or deceased."

### "Inspired By" vs. "Is" -- Does It Matter?

Legally, the label matters less than the **substance and public perception**. If a reasonable user would understand the tool as simulating Steve Jobs' persona, calling it "inspired by" provides limited protection. However, if the tool genuinely abstracts principles into a generic framework and uses Jobs as one cited source among several, "inspired by" creates meaningful legal distance.

### Historical Figures (50+ Years Deceased)

Generally **safer** because:
- California's 70-year post-mortem right of publicity still applies, but older estates are less likely to have active legal representation monitoring for violations
- Cultural figures become increasingly part of the public domain of ideas over time
- Fewer commercial interests at stake

For Jobs (died 2011), the estate is active, well-funded, and the brand remains commercially valuable. This is meaningfully riskier than using, say, Henry Ford (d. 1947) or Thomas Edison (d. 1931).

## 5. Steve Jobs Estate -- Specific Risk Assessment

### The Estate Structure

The Steven P. Jobs Trust passed entirely to **Laurene Powell Jobs**, who has sole control. The estate was structured for complete privacy -- no probate, no public inventory. Powell Jobs' net worth exceeds $10 billion.

### Litigiousness Assessment: LOW-TO-MODERATE

Based on research findings:
- **No known lawsuits** have been filed by the Jobs estate over likeness, name, or persona issues
- The estate has been described as "drama-free" compared to estates like James Brown's or Tupac's
- Powell Jobs has publicly stated she is not focused on legacy wealth accumulation
- Powell Jobs' focus is on philanthropy (Emerson Collective) rather than brand enforcement

### The Steve Jobs Biopics -- Precedent

Multiple Jobs biopics were produced:
- **Jobs (2013)** starring Ashton Kutcher -- produced without estate cooperation
- **Steve Jobs (2015)** directed by Danny Boyle, written by Aaron Sorkin -- based on Isaacson biography rights (acquired from the publisher, not the estate)

Neither film required formal estate permission. The films relied on:
1. First Amendment protections for biographical/expressive works about public figures
2. Rights acquired from the Isaacson biography (a work Jobs himself authorized but did not control)
3. Consultation with real people depicted (Wozniak, Sculley, etc.)

The estate did not sue over either film, despite the 2015 film containing significant fictionalized elements.

### Apple Inc. Consideration

Apple may independently have interests in Jobs' name/brand, though the nature of any IP agreements between Jobs and Apple is not publicly known. Apple has historically been protective of its trademarks and brand.

## 6. Risk Assessment Summary

| Approach | Risk Level | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| AI chatbot that "is" Steve Jobs (uses name, first person, simulates personality) | **HIGH** | Right of publicity claim likely viable; George Carlin precedent applies |
| AI that "thinks like" Steve Jobs (uses name, third person, applies framework) | **MEDIUM** | Name use triggers 3344.1; "public perception" test is key risk |
| Named framework citing Jobs as source ("Jobs-inspired product framework") | **MEDIUM-LOW** | Attribution =/= impersonation; scholarly/analytical framing helps |
| Abstract framework derived from Jobs' principles (no name in product) | **LOW** | Ideas/methods not copyrightable; no name = no publicity right trigger |
| Framework citing multiple thinkers including Jobs as one source | **LOW** | Analytical/scholarly use; transformative; no single persona simulated |

### Factors That INCREASE Risk
- Using "Steve Jobs" in the product name, marketing, or UI
- First-person voice ("I believe..." as Jobs)
- Generating novel opinions attributed to Jobs on topics he never addressed
- Monetizing the product commercially (vs. educational/research use)
- Reproducing lengthy copyrighted text from biographies or interviews

### Factors That DECREASE Risk
- Framing as analytical framework, not persona simulation
- Clear disclaimers of non-affiliation
- Using Jobs as one cited source among many
- Focusing on documented, publicly available principles
- Educational or scholarly context
- Not replicating voice or visual likeness
- Attributing ideas to their published sources

---

## 7. Key Legal Gaps and Uncertainties

1. **No case law directly addresses** whether a text-based AI reasoning framework (without voice/visual replication) constitutes a "digital replica" under California law
2. **No federal right of publicity exists** yet -- the NO FAKES Act remains pending
3. **The "public perception" test** for distinguishing inspiration from impersonation has no clear judicial standard
4. **Fair use boundaries** for AI-extracted principles from copyrighted biographies are untested
5. **The Jobs estate's enforcement posture** regarding AI specifically is unknown
