SOURCE: Autobiography of Andrew Carnegie (published 1920, posthumously)

The Autobiography is itself a primary artifact of Carnegie's decision-making style — a curated self-narrative composed in his final decade in which he selected which episodes to remember, in what order, and with what causal connective tissue. The document is therefore evidence not merely of what Carnegie did but of how he wanted future decision-makers to understand the logic by which he had operated. Reading it as a CDM source means reading past the warm reminiscence to the underlying theory of action it preserves.

The most striking decision-pattern visible across the text is what might be called retrospective consequence-chaining: Carnegie habitually narrates moments as branching points whose downstream value compounded over decades, rather than as isolated tactical wins. The Adams Express stock purchase that Tom Scott helped finance is described as the "first dollar I ever made without working for it" — but Carnegie's emphasis is not on the cash dividend but on what the experience taught him about capital that earns capital, an insight he then describes as the foundation for every subsequent investment decision. He is, in effect, reasoning about the meta-strategic value of an early experience rather than its direct payoff.

The Autobiography reveals a consistent decision-evaluation framework in which Carnegie weighted each opportunity along three axes: (1) its potential to become an information advantage in another arena, (2) its capacity to generate compounding cash flows that could be redeployed into yet another opportunity, and (3) its impact on his network position relative to figures whose patronage he was actively cultivating. He almost never describes a decision in terms of its standalone merit. Each move is staged in relation to the next move it enables.

A second visible pattern is selective reframing of failure. Where the historical record shows Carnegie made losing bets — the Storey Farm oil investment, the Pullman sleeping-car maneuvers — the Autobiography either presents these as instructive learning episodes or omits the most embarrassing details. This is not idle vanity; it reveals his operational stance toward setbacks. A loss was an input into the cost-of-information ledger, not a referendum on judgment. He moved on quickly because he treated the question "what does this teach me about my next move" as more decision-relevant than "what does this say about my previous one."

A third pattern is the heavy weight Carnegie places on the earliest moments — Dunfermline weaving sheds, Pittsburgh telegraph office, the bobbin boy in the cotton mill. He returns repeatedly to the period before he had capital and reconstructs how scarcity disciplined his attention to costs. His later cost obsession in steel was, by his own account, an extrapolation of the cognitive habits that scarcity forced upon him as a child. The Autobiography stages Carnegie's decision-making system as an unbroken line from those early constraints to the multi-million-dollar capital allocations of his Pittsburgh years — the same discipline applied at increasing scales.

Notably, Carnegie's account of the labor conflicts of the 1880s and 1890s — including Homestead — is comparatively thin and defensive, suggesting that even in self-narration he could not coherently fold those events into the consequence-chaining framework that organizes the rest of the book. This silence is itself a CDM signal: when the narrative coherence breaks down at a specific class of decision, that signals the constructs at work in successful decisions did not extend cleanly into that domain.

The Autobiography also reveals Carnegie's distinctive treatment of mentors and patrons as deliberately constructed dependencies. He writes about Scott, about Anderson and his library, about Mrs. Stewart, in terms that emphasize the asymmetry of value he extracted from each relationship — modest returns to the mentor, compounding returns to himself. He does not present this with cynicism; he presents it as the natural moral arithmetic of patronage well-used. The lesson he means to transmit to future readers is that one should engineer one's own exposure to people whose marginal generosity costs them little but yields disproportionate returns to oneself.

Finally, the Autobiography functions as a manifesto in disguise: by selecting which episodes to memorialize, Carnegie is implicitly recommending a decision-making system to the reader. The system has a recognizable structure: notice unit costs and compounding leverage where others see only momentary opportunity; cultivate patrons with low-marginal-cost generosity; redeploy small wins into larger structural positions before competitors realize the position is contestable; treat losses as inputs to a learning loop rather than as evaluations of self; and design one's life around the question of which moves enable the next move rather than which moves maximize the immediate one.

The text should be read as the curated source code of Carnegie's decision architecture — incomplete in places, selectively reconstructed, but consistent in the underlying logic it preserves.
