SOURCE: "Wealth" (June 1889, North American Review), later republished as "The Gospel of Wealth"

The Gospel of Wealth is the most explicit statement Carnegie ever published of the decision-framework he applied to capital allocation in the second half of his life. It deserves CDM analysis not as ethics but as a piece of strategic doctrine: a public commitment that constrained his subsequent options and thereby pre-committed him to a specific class of decisions.

The doctrine's central operational claim is that surplus wealth in the hands of one person is a trustee position rather than a property right. Carnegie does not argue this from theology or sentiment; he argues it as a consequentialist calculation about social stability. His claim is that the alternative dispositions of large fortunes — bequest to heirs, return to communities through inheritance taxes, scattered direct charity — are all systematically inferior to a centralized, intentional re-deployment of wealth by the man who acquired it. This is an argument about decision quality across alternative trustees, not an argument about justice.

The decision-pattern this reveals is striking. Carnegie applied the same comparative-advantage reasoning to philanthropy that he applied to vertical integration in steel: identify which agent in the system can most efficiently process a given resource, and route the resource to that agent regardless of conventional ownership claims. In the steel context, the most efficient processor was Carnegie Steel because of cost discipline. In the wealth-distribution context, the most efficient processor of accumulated capital was — in Carnegie's view — the original accumulator, because he alone had the structural understanding of how capital had been generated and could most accurately judge how it should be re-deployed.

The doctrine also embeds a sharp ranking of philanthropic targets. Carnegie's seven preferred uses — universities, libraries, hospitals, public parks, halls for music, public swimming baths, and church buildings — share a common feature: they are non-rivalrous infrastructure whose value compounds over generations as more users access them at near-zero marginal cost. Direct relief to the poor — almsgiving — is explicitly ranked at the bottom because it is consumed in a single cycle and, in Carnegie's assessment, often reinforces the conditions that produced the need for relief.

This ranking reveals a CDM pattern that recurs across Carnegie's commercial decisions: prefer investments whose value persists and compounds over those whose value is consumed. The Bessemer rolling mill that produced steel rails for thirty years was preferred to a windfall contract that paid out once. The library system that produced readers for a century was preferred to a soup kitchen that fed people for a winter. The unit of value Carnegie tracked was not the immediate benefit but the integrated benefit over the asset's productive life.

The doctrine also contains a sharp constraint on timing: the rich man should give while alive, not at death. Carnegie's argument is that posthumous bequests evade the discipline of having to choose well — the donor never sees the consequences of his gift and therefore never refines his giving. He calls bequests "the Bequest is the most fitting monument when it cannot be otherwise" — i.e., a fallback only when active stewardship has failed. The decision-implication is that wealth-disposition is itself a skill that requires feedback loops, and that posthumous giving severs the feedback loop and therefore systematically degrades decision quality.

Carnegie's doctrine functions, as a piece of strategic decision architecture, as a commitment device. By publishing it in 1889 — when he was 53 and the bulk of his fortune still ahead of him — he structurally pre-committed to disposing of the fortune. This is consistent with his pattern of using public commitments to lock in future actions whose long-term value exceeded short-term cost. The Concordat-style move: announce the form of the disposition before the fortune is even fully realized, making subsequent retreat from the doctrine more reputationally expensive than carrying it through.

A subtler pattern visible in the doctrine is Carnegie's preference for matching mechanisms. Throughout the text he emphasizes that recipients should not be passive — communities receiving libraries should provide land, maintenance, and operating funds; institutions receiving endowments should demonstrate organizational capacity. This is the same engineering logic that animated his steel partnerships: the recipient must have skin in the game, must be structurally co-invested, or the gift becomes a liability that erodes the capacity it was meant to build.

Finally, the doctrine reveals what Carnegie did not select as primary giving targets: political reform, regulatory advocacy, redistributive taxation. These are absent. He preferred infrastructure to advocacy, ladders to safety nets, capability to entitlement. This is a decision-pattern, not an ethical lapse: he believed structural infrastructure routing capability through dispersed individuals would do more decision-relevant work over time than centralized institutional reform, even where the latter was the more direct response to a problem.

The Gospel of Wealth should therefore be read not as Carnegie's ethics but as the formal specification of his second-half capital allocation strategy — a strategy whose structure mirrored the first-half strategy in steel, applied to a different kind of asset and audience.
