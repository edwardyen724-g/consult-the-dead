Letters to Generals: Lincoln Diagnosing Strategic Reasoning Failures
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from the Collected Works (Basler, ed., vols. 5–8) and
the Lincoln/McClellan, Lincoln/Hooker, Lincoln/Meade, and Lincoln/Grant correspondence.

== THE LETTERS AS DECISION-MAKING DOCUMENTS ==

Lincoln's correspondence with his generals is among the richest documentary record of
his strategic thinking, because in these letters he is doing two things simultaneously:
giving operational direction and explaining the reasoning a competent commander should
have already done. When he writes to a general, he reveals the criteria by which he is
evaluating the general's decisions. The letters function as Lincoln's own working notes
on what military judgment requires.

== THE UNSENT McCLELLAN LETTER (APRIL 9, 1862) ==

After McClellan complained that his force was inadequate and that the administration was
withholding troops, Lincoln wrote a long reply that opens: "Your dispatches complaining
that you are not properly sustained, while they do not offer suchcomplaintexplicitly,
do really impose so." He then itemizes McClellan's actual force levels, the location and
mission of every withheld unit, and the operational logic of the Washington defense. The
letter reads like a forensic deconstruction of McClellan's reasoning frame: "And, once
more let me tell you, it is indispensable to you that you strike a blow." The decision
signal here is that Lincoln treated McClellan's complaint as a category error — the
general was solving the wrong problem (justifying inaction by reference to enemy strength)
rather than the right problem (acting against the available enemy with the available force).

== THE HOOKER LETTER (JANUARY 26, 1863) ==

The Hooker letter is the most analytically dense single document in the collection. Lincoln
appoints Hooker to command the Army of the Potomac and immediately tells him what is
wrong with him: ambitious to a fault, having undermined Burnside, having said the country
needed a dictator. Then: "Only those generals who gain successes, can set up dictators."
And: "Beware of rashness. Beware of rashness, but with energy, and sleepless vigilance,
go forward, and give us victories." The letter shows Lincoln explicitly modeling a
character flaw, naming it, and converting it into operational guidance. He does not ask
Hooker to be different; he asks him to use the energy of his ambition while watching
for its predictable failure mode.

== THE MEADE LETTER AFTER GETTYSBURG (JULY 14, 1863) ==

After Meade allowed Lee's army to escape across the Potomac following Gettysburg, Lincoln
wrote a letter expressing his "deep distress" — and then did not send it. The unsent
letter reasons: Lee was within Meade's grasp, the war could have ended, the chance was
lost. The decision to write but not send is itself a documented Lincoln practice. He used
writing as a way to externalize and discharge anger so that subsequent action would not
be driven by it. The pattern recurs across the war years: the letter is composed, the
analysis is preserved, the explosive expression is not transmitted. The technique converts
the emotion into a decision input rather than a decision driver.

== THE GRANT CORRESPONDENCE ==

With Grant, Lincoln's letters change in tone. Where the McClellan letters explain, the
Grant letters confirm. The April 30, 1864 letter on the eve of the Wilderness campaign:
"The particulars of your plan I neither know, or seek to know. You are vigilant and
self-reliant; and, pleased with this, I wish not to obtrude any constraints or restraints
upon you." This is a remarkable document: Lincoln tells his commanding general that he
is deliberately staying out of operational planning. He had calibrated which commander
needed which kind of supervision. Where McClellan needed pressure to act, Grant needed
freedom to act. The selection rule: match the supervisory style to the failure mode of
the subordinate.

== WHAT THE LETTERS REVEAL AS A SET ==

Read together, the letters show a consistent decision-making instrument. Lincoln evaluates
his generals not on outcomes but on the soundness of their decision processes — whether
they reason about whole theaters or individual battles, whether they treat absent
information as licensing inaction, whether they confuse caution with prudence. He believes
strategic judgment is a teachable skill but only inside a person who is already willing
to be wrong in public. Generals who could not survive the public exposure of error were
generals he eventually replaced. He treated the war as a sequence of selection decisions
on personnel, with operational guidance as an interim diagnostic to inform the selection.
