Public Opinion as Operating Constraint: The Radius of Action Theory
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Donald (1995), Boritt (1978), and Lincoln's own
statements on public opinion in speeches, letters, and the 1858 Lincoln-Douglas debates.

== THE EXPLICIT THEORY ==

In a December 1859 letter and again in his speeches, Lincoln articulated a position he
would maintain throughout his presidency: "Public sentiment is everything. With public
sentiment, nothing can fail; without it nothing can succeed. Consequently he who moulds
public sentiment, goes deeper than he who enacts statutes or pronounces decisions. He
makes statutes and decisions possible or impossible to be executed." This is not a
descriptive observation. It is a strategic doctrine: public opinion is the upstream
variable, and policy is the downstream consequence. The implication is that a leader
who acts ahead of opinion succeeds only if he has already moved opinion, and a leader
who waits for opinion always succeeds, but always too late on the issues that matter.

== THE RADIUS OF ACTION ==

David Donald's framing — that Lincoln operated within a "radius of action" set by public
opinion and worked deliberately to expand it — captures the operational implication.
Lincoln's pattern: identify the maximum distance opinion could be moved in a given period,
move toward the policy preference but not past the radius, and use communication to
expand the radius before the next move. Acting beyond the radius was not principled; it
was politically destructive in a way that would set the cause back. Acting well inside
the radius was a missed opportunity. The optimal action is at the edge of the achievable.

== THE EMANCIPATION TIMING AS RADIUS MANAGEMENT ==

The two-year delay before the Emancipation Proclamation reflected this theory directly.
Lincoln did not need to be persuaded of slavery's wrong. He needed Northern public
opinion to be persuaded that emancipation was a war necessity rather than a Radical
demand. Each Confederate atrocity, each loss in battle, each Border State refusal to
accept compensated emancipation, expanded the radius. By September 1862 the radius had
expanded to the point where emancipation could be issued as a war measure without
fracturing the Union coalition. The signal: he was tracking opinion movement against a
mental threshold and acting when the threshold was crossed.

== THE COSTS OF THE THEORY ==

Acting on this theory imposes a moral cost: people remain enslaved longer than the
president's preferences would dictate. Lincoln accepted this cost as the price of the
larger goal. He did not pretend the cost was zero. To Horace Greeley in August 1862:
"If I could save the Union without freeing any slave I would do it, and if I could save
it by freeing all the slaves I would do it; and if I could save it by freeing some and
leaving others alone I would also do that." Greeley took this as evidence Lincoln was
indifferent to slavery. The careful reading: Lincoln is publicly anchoring himself to
the legitimating frame (Union preservation) while preserving operational flexibility
to do whatever the radius allows. He is teaching the public to accept emancipation as
a Union-saving measure.

== EXPANDING RADIUS THROUGH RHETORICAL POSITIONING ==

The Gettysburg Address (November 19, 1863) is partly an exercise in radius expansion.
By framing the war as a dedication to "the proposition that all men are created equal,"
Lincoln retroactively redefined the war's purpose and shifted the public sentiment
about what victory must include. The 272-word address did the work that policy could
not: it moved opinion to a position from which the 13th Amendment could later be
politically achievable. The Cooper Union speech (1860), the First Inaugural, the Annual
Messages — each is a rhetorical instrument designed to expand the radius for the next
policy step.

== WHAT THE THEORY REVEALS AS A METHOD ==

The radius doctrine has a structural implication for any decision: the question is never
"what is right?" alone, nor "what is achievable?" alone, but "what is the maximum
movement toward the right that is currently achievable, and what communication would
expand that maximum next quarter?" Action and rhetoric are coupled instruments. Lincoln
treated speeches as policy moves and policy moves as rhetorical setups for future
action. The unit of analysis is never a single decision; it is a sequence of decisions
inside a moving constraint that the leader is partially responsible for moving. This is
a fundamentally different decision frame from utilitarian optimization or principled
constancy. It is closer to a control theory of leadership: small adjustments at the edge
of the feasible, with feedback through communication, sustained over time.
