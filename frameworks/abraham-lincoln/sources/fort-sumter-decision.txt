Fort Sumter: The First Executive Decision and the Inheritance of War Initiation
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from McPherson (Battle Cry of Freedom), Nicolay & Hay,
the Collected Works, and the cabinet correspondence of March-April 1861.

== THE PROBLEM AS LINCOLN INHERITED IT ==

When Lincoln took office on March 4, 1861, seven states had seceded and Major Anderson
held Fort Sumter in Charleston harbor with provisions for roughly six weeks. Buchanan had
left no policy. Lincoln's options reduced to four: evacuate Sumter (which Scott and most
of the cabinet initially recommended), reinforce militarily, resupply with food only, or
trade Sumter for some concession. Each option was a different way of assigning responsibility
for the war's initiation. Lincoln understood that the meaning of any move would be set
not by what was done but by who appeared to choose violence.

== THE CABINET POLLING (MARCH 15 AND MARCH 29) ==

Lincoln asked his cabinet twice in writing whether to provision Sumter. On March 15 only
Blair favored it. By March 29 the count had shifted toward provisioning. Lincoln did not
follow either majority. The polling was not a vote-counting exercise. He used it to surface
the strongest arguments on each side, observe how cabinet members reasoned, and identify
which had thought through second-order consequences. Seward — who wanted to evacuate
and proposed a foreign-war-as-distraction memo on April 1 — failed this test conspicuously,
and the failure permanently realigned the internal hierarchy of the cabinet.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

Lincoln's chosen course was to send a resupply expedition carrying food only, and to notify
South Carolina governor Pickens in advance. The notification was the key tactical move.
By telling Pickens what was coming, Lincoln transferred the next decision to the Confederacy:
they could allow food to reach hungry soldiers, or they could fire on a humanitarian
mission. Either choice favored the Union politically. If they allowed it, Sumter held
without conflict and Lincoln gained time. If they fired, the war began with the Confederacy
visibly initiating violence against an unarmed relief mission. The structure of the move
constrained the opponent into a forced error.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE FRAMING ==

Lincoln had told Congress, and would tell the country in his July 4 message, that "the
Union must be preserved" — but he was acutely aware that the legitimacy of preservation
depended on its appearing to be defensive. To act first was to legitimize secession's
counter-narrative that the North was the aggressor. To do nothing was to ratify secession
by inaction. The resupply expedition resolved both problems: it acted, but it acted in
a way that made any violent escalation the choice of someone else. The decision pattern
shows Lincoln's instinct for asymmetric framing: when both action and inaction carry
costs, restructure the situation so that the moral cost of escalation falls on the
opponent.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

After Sumter fell on April 13, Lincoln told Orville Browning in private that the plan
"succeeded; they attacked Sumter — it fell, and thus did more service than it otherwise
could." Some historians read this as cynical. The more careful reading is that Lincoln
treated the fort's loss as instrumentally costly but strategically clarifying. He had
chosen a course in which both outcomes — Sumter holding peacefully or Sumter falling
militarily — left the Union politically stronger than the alternatives. He optimized
not for the best outcome but for the worst-case outcome.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

Sumter is the cleanest documented example of Lincoln's decision pattern under high
uncertainty: define what cannot be allowed (the dissolution of the Union), accept that
he cannot determine the outcome, and structure the action so that the unfavorable
counterparty is forced to make the morally costly choice. He did not try to predict
Davis's response; he engineered a situation where any of Davis's responses produced
acceptable outcomes for the Union. The technique recurs throughout the war years —
in the Trent affair, in negotiations with Border States, in handling Radical Republicans,
in the Hampton Roads conference — and is identifiable as a coherent method.
