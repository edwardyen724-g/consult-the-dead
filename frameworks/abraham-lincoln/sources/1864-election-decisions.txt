The 1864 Election: Decisions When Defeat Seemed Likely
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from McPherson, Goodwin, Donald, and the documentary
record of Lincoln's August 1864 "blind memorandum" and the Hampton Roads conference
of February 1865.

== THE PROBLEM IN AUGUST 1864 ==

By midsummer 1864, the Union war effort had stalled. Grant's Overland Campaign had
suffered enormous casualties without breaking Lee's army. Sherman was still outside
Atlanta. Lincoln's renomination by the Republicans (rebranded as the National Union
Party) had been contested. The Democratic platform, drafted in late August at Chicago,
declared the war a failure and called for an immediate cessation of hostilities. The
Democratic nominee, George McClellan, repudiated parts of the platform but accepted
the nomination. By all available political indicators, Lincoln expected to lose.

== THE BLIND MEMORANDUM (AUGUST 23, 1864) ==

On August 23, 1864, Lincoln wrote a memorandum which he then folded and sealed and
asked each cabinet member to sign on the back without reading. The text read: "This
morning, as for some days past, it seems exceedingly probable that this Administration
will not be re-elected. Then it will be my duty to so co-operate with the President
elect, as to save the Union between the election and the inauguration; as he will
have secured his election on such ground that he can not possibly save it afterwards."
He revealed the contents only after his eventual reelection.

== WHAT THE MEMO DEMONSTRATES ==

The memorandum is the most precise documented instance of Lincoln making a forward
commitment under expected adverse conditions. He bound himself in writing to a
specific course of action — full cooperation with a successor he expected to abandon
the war — and required cabinet witnessing. The act has multiple decision-method
implications. (1) He assumed his own future judgment under post-defeat emotional
conditions might not be reliable, and pre-committed to constrain it. (2) He
distinguished between his political defeat and the Union's survival, and prioritized
the latter. (3) He bound the cabinet as well as himself, anticipating that the
question of cooperation with McClellan would be contested even by his own administration.

== THE PRE-COMMITMENT PATTERN ==

The blind memo is not isolated. It is one of several documented pre-commitments
Lincoln made when he anticipated future emotional or political conditions might
distort judgment. The Antietam-trigger pre-commitment for the Emancipation Proclamation
is another. The unsent letters are another (he externalized the heat in writing,
then committed to not transmitting). The early polling of his cabinet in March 1861
on Sumter, before the situation had clarified, was a means of having considered
arguments before crisis pressure made calm consideration harder. The pattern: when
the future will be emotionally costly, lock in the reasoning now.

== HAMPTON ROADS (FEBRUARY 3, 1865) ==

Six months after the August memo, conditions had reversed. Atlanta had fallen in
early September; Sherman's march had broken Confederate logistics; Lincoln had won
reelection comfortably; the 13th Amendment had passed the House. Confederate
commissioners (Stephens, Hunter, Campbell) approached for peace negotiations. Lincoln
went personally to Hampton Roads to meet them. He made the war's terms explicit:
restoration of national authority, no recession from emancipation, disbandment of
hostile forces. The commissioners proposed a possible armistice followed by joint
action against French intervention in Mexico. Lincoln declined. The decision was
to accept no settlement that left the union question or the slavery question
unresolved, even at the price of continued war.

== THE COMPENSATION PROPOSAL ==

A revealing detail from the post-Hampton Roads period: Lincoln drafted a proposal to
Congress to appropriate $400 million for compensation to Southern slaveholders if
the rebellion ceased and the 13th Amendment was ratified. He showed it to his cabinet
on February 5, 1865. The cabinet uniformly opposed it. Lincoln pocketed the proposal
and remarked that he was satisfied "you are all opposed to me." The episode shows
Lincoln willing to offer extraordinary financial concessions to end the war on terms
that secured the structural goals (Union, abolition). It also shows him accepting
unanimous cabinet opposition without overriding it — using the cabinet as an
information instrument rather than an obstacle.

== THE METHOD VISIBLE ACROSS 1864 ==

Across 1864, Lincoln operated under an inverted optimism profile: he expected defeat,
prepared for defeat, and acted as if his time was limited. The behavior under that
expectation is informative. He pushed for the 13th Amendment in the harder pre-
inauguration window. He pre-committed to Union-saving cooperation with his expected
successor. He continued to write candidly to allies and enemies (the Conkling letter,
August 26, 1864, defending emancipation publicly to Northern Democrats). He did not
lower his standards in pursuit of electoral survival. The discipline visible here is
the operating principle: act on the goal regardless of personal political prospect,
because the political prospect is downstream of the goal's progress, not a separate
input.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING POSTURE ==

The 1864 decisions are the cleanest example of Lincoln making consequential choices
while expecting his career to end. The choices made under that condition — pushing
for irreversible policy, pre-committing himself in writing, refusing settlements that
would leave structural questions unresolved — are the choices of someone who has
internalized that durable structural change is more important than personal political
survival. The posture is the operating premise of his framework. Once seen, it makes
sense of dozens of smaller decisions that look puzzling under a self-preservation
assumption: he was optimizing for outcomes that would outlast him, and he treated
his own continued tenure as a means rather than an end.
