General Selection and Removal: Lincoln's Personnel Decision Pattern (1861-1864)
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from McPherson, Donald, Goodwin, and the operational
correspondence record of the Army of the Potomac and the Western theater commanders.

== THE PROBLEM SPACE ==

Lincoln took office having never commanded troops. He had to evaluate, select, and remove
generals while simultaneously learning the criteria by which generals should be evaluated.
The Army of the Potomac alone went through six commanders before Grant: McDowell, McClellan,
Burnside, Hooker, Meade, and Grant. Each transition was a documented decision. The pattern
across them is more revealing than any single removal.

== McCLELLAN: THE TWO-FAILURE THRESHOLD ==

McClellan was Lincoln's first major personnel investment and his most painful firing.
The pattern Lincoln applied: he tolerated McClellan's contempt (Lincoln waiting in
McClellan's parlor in November 1861 while McClellan went to bed), he tolerated his
political opposition, he tolerated his slowness during the Peninsula campaign, and he
even reinstated him after Pope's defeat at Second Bull Run because there was no
operationally adequate replacement. He fired McClellan only after Antietam, when McClellan
again failed to pursue a defeated Lee. The decision rule: tolerate failure of personality
indefinitely, tolerate failure of execution once, fire after the second strategic failure
of the same type.

== BURNSIDE AND HOOKER: REPLACEMENT WITHOUT IDEAL OPTIONS ==

Burnside replaced McClellan, fought Fredericksburg disastrously, and was replaced by
Hooker. Hooker fought Chancellorsville disastrously and was replaced by Meade three days
before Gettysburg. Lincoln did not wait for ideal replacements. He fired commanders
into a situation where the available successor was clearly inadequate, accepting the
operational risk because the cost of retaining a known failure outranked the cost of
trying an unknown alternative. The rule embedded here: a known failure is more dangerous
than an unknown variable; replace, do not optimize.

== THE LIFTING OF GRANT'S ENEMIES ==

Grant came under repeated attack for his alleged drinking, his casualty rates after
Shiloh, and the apparent stalemate around Vicksburg. Lincoln received explicit demands
for Grant's removal from political figures, military rivals, and newspaper editors. He
declined each one with variants of: "I can't spare this man — he fights." The criterion
he was applying: identify the variable that distinguishes the rare effective commander
from the common ineffective one, and weight all other variables (politics, manners,
public opinion) below that variable. Grant attacked. That was the variable Lincoln had
identified as scarce and load-bearing. Everything else was negotiable.

== HOW LINCOLN ASSESSED GENERALS ==

The criteria visible in his correspondence and conversation: (1) Did the general act on
available information rather than wait for complete information? (2) Did the general
accept losses as the cost of battle, or treat any loss as a reason to retreat? (3) Did
the general reason about whole theaters or about his own command's safety? (4) Did the
general communicate clearly with civilian leadership, or use reporting as political
positioning? (5) Did the general improve, or repeat the same errors? Lincoln did not
expect generals to be tactically brilliant. He expected them to clear a low bar reliably:
fight, communicate, learn.

== THE EMOTIONAL CONTAINMENT PATTERN ==

Across these decisions, Lincoln's documented emotional pattern is striking. He did not
publicly criticize the generals he removed. He did not write angry letters that arrived
at their destinations. The famous unsent Meade letter is one of many. The principle:
the public language of removals must preserve the dignity of the institution and the
removed officer, because the next replacement will fail too, and the cost of demoralizing
the officer corps exceeds the satisfaction of public criticism. He maintained the working
relationships even with men whose performance had failed.

== WHAT THE PATTERN REVEALS ==

Lincoln treated personnel as the operational variable he could most directly control.
Strategy was contingent on commander capacity; commander capacity was contingent on
selection; selection was a sequence of imperfect bets. The framework: identify the
scarce, load-bearing trait (willingness to fight, in this domain), select on that trait
even at the cost of other traits, replace decisively when failure of that trait becomes
visible, accept that some replacements will also fail, and treat the entire activity
as an iterative search rather than a one-shot decision. This same operating method —
optimize on one load-bearing variable, accept secondary failures — recurs across Lincoln's
non-military decisions as well.
