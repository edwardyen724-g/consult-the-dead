Fragment on Government (c. 1854): First Principles in Private
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — analysis of the short fragment in the Collected Works (vol. 2,
pp. 220–222), with reference to Donald (1995) and Boritt (1978) on Lincoln's working method.

== THE DOCUMENT ==

The "Fragment on Government" is a short, undated, unpublished document found among
Lincoln's papers, conjecturally dated to mid-1854 by Roy Basler on internal evidence
linking it to the Kansas-Nebraska debate period. It opens with a single sentence that
becomes the load-bearing premise: "The legitimate object of government, is to do for
a community of people, whatever they need to have done, but can not do, at all, or
can not so well do, for themselves — in their separate, and individual capacities."
The fragment then enumerates examples (defense against violence, public roads, public
schools, public charities) and identifies their common feature: they are activities
in which the collective is more efficient than the individual.

== WHAT THE PRIVATE-NESS REVEALS ==

The document was never published. There is no evidence Lincoln showed it to anyone.
It reads as a working note — a man writing down a definition for his own clarity. The
practice is revealing. Lincoln habitually composed private documents in which he
worked out first principles before applying them publicly. The Meditation on the
Divine Will (1862), the Fragment on the Constitution and the Union (c. 1860), the
Fragment on Slavery (c. 1854), the Fragment on Niagara Falls (c. 1848), the Fragment
on Sectionalism (1856), the Fragment on Pro-Slavery Theology (1858) — all are
private working notes in the same genre. They show a habit of crystallizing principle
in writing before relying on it in argument.

== THE LOGICAL STRUCTURE ==

The fragment's argument is a definition followed by a derivation followed by a class
of examples. The definition: government's legitimate scope. The derivation: any function
that fails the test (individuals can do it themselves, well) is outside government's
proper scope. The examples: defense, public works, public goods. The structure is
characteristic of Lincoln's reasoning: a single load-bearing definition followed by
a derived rule followed by tests of the rule against cases. He uses the same structure
in his constitutional reasoning ("preserving the Union" defined first, derivations
applied to specific cases) and in his strategic reasoning ("attack the South's capacity
to wage war" defined first, derivations applied to theaters and campaigns).

== WHY THE DEFINITION DOES UNUSUAL WORK ==

Most political definitions of government's purpose are normative claims (government
should do X because it is right). Lincoln's definition is functional: government's
legitimate role is determined by an efficiency comparison between collective and
individual action. The framing has consequences. It treats government as an
instrument with a comparative advantage, not as a moral end. It is silent on questions
of distributive justice. It implies that the proper scope of government is empirical
— it expands when collective action is more efficient and contracts when it is not.
This is a working definition for a politician who needs to make case-by-case decisions
about which interventions are warranted, not a philosophical treatise for posterity.

== THE COMPATIBILITY WITH LINCOLN'S POLICY POSITIONS ==

The fragment's definition is compatible with the entire range of Lincoln's policy
commitments. Internal improvements: roads, canals, and railroads are activities in
which the collective is more efficient than the individual (high capital costs, network
effects). Public schools: education has positive externalities individuals cannot
fully internalize. Tariffs: the collective negotiation of trade rules cannot be done
by individuals separately. The Homestead Act: federal land distribution is the only
mechanism that can settle public land at scale. The Pacific Railway: continental
infrastructure required collective financing. Each policy is a test case in which the
fragment's rule produces the position Lincoln in fact took.

== THE FRAGMENT AS A LENS FOR DECISION RECONSTRUCTION ==

For framework reconstruction, the fragment is unusually informative because it documents
the operating principle Lincoln was applying without the political language that
ordinarily obscures it. When he made decisions about war powers, about intervention
in state policy, about federal regulation of the economy, the fragment's test
condition is a candidate explanation for the position he chose. The fragment also
illuminates what he did not include: there is no theology, no Founders-citation, no
appeal to natural rights. The legitimacy of government is grounded in a comparative-
advantage argument, not a transcendent one. This grounding is the load-bearing logic
under most of his political reasoning.

== METHOD VISIBLE IN THE PRACTICE OF FRAGMENTS ==

The broader pattern — Lincoln's practice of writing private fragments to clarify his
own thinking — is itself a methodological signal. He treated his cognition as something
that needed to be externalized in writing before he could trust it. Speaking from
prepared text, writing draft documents, composing letters and not sending them: all
are instances of the same habit. The conviction is that thinking-in-writing is more
reliable than thinking-in-speech, and that public claims should be tested first by
private articulation. The fragment on government is one of the cleanest documented
examples of this habit applied to the most foundational question.
