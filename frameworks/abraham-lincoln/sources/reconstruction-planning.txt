Reconstruction Planning: Decision-Making Under Anticipated Conditions
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from McPherson, Foner's "Reconstruction" (1988), the
December 1863 Proclamation of Amnesty and Reconstruction, the July 1864 Wade-Davis
veto, and the April 11, 1865 final speech.

== THE PROBLEM ==

By December 1863, with Vicksburg and Gettysburg won and the war's outcome no longer
in doubt at major-power scale, Lincoln faced a new decision class: under what conditions
should seceded states be readmitted, and under whose authority should that decision
be made? The question was both legal (were they ever truly out of the Union?) and
practical (what mechanism would produce stable post-war governance?). Congress and
the executive each claimed primary authority. Radical Republicans wanted strict
conditions; Lincoln wanted lenient ones. The decision was made under conditions of
high uncertainty about what the post-war South would look like.

== THE DECEMBER 1863 PROCLAMATION ==

The Proclamation of Amnesty and Reconstruction offered pardon to most former Confederates
who took an oath of loyalty, and authorized state governments to be reconstituted when
10 percent of the 1860 voting population had taken the oath and ratified emancipation.
The "ten percent plan" was deliberately lenient. Lincoln chose it because it was a
threshold low enough that loyal minorities could plausibly cross it within a reasonable
timeframe, providing a procedural anchor for re-incorporation. The criterion was
not optimality. The criterion was achievability. Lincoln preferred a flawed but
operational mechanism to an ideal but unworkable one.

== THE WADE-DAVIS COLLISION (JULY 1864) ==

Congress responded with the Wade-Davis Bill, which required 50 percent loyalty oaths,
ironclad oaths from all officials, and stricter conditions on emancipation enforcement.
Lincoln pocket-vetoed the bill — a nearly unprecedented use of pocket veto on major
legislation. He then issued a proclamation explaining his veto: he was unwilling to
commit himself "inflexibly" to a single restoration plan, preferring to retain
flexibility to apply the most workable mechanism state by state. The decision pattern:
preserve optionality across cases, refuse to bind himself to a uniform rule when the
domain is heterogeneous.

== THE TENNESSEE AND LOUISIANA CASES ==

Lincoln applied the Reconstruction framework first in Tennessee (under Andrew Johnson
as military governor) and Louisiana (where 10-percent governments formed in 1864).
The Louisiana case was particularly contested because the new state government refused
to extend voting rights to Black men. Lincoln's response, in the April 11, 1865 speech
(his last public address), was characteristically careful: he supported the new
government as imperfect but operational, while expressing his own preference that
suffrage be extended at least to "the very intelligent, and on those who serve our
cause as soldiers." This is the first public endorsement by an American president of
limited Black suffrage. He did not impose it; he expressed a preference within a
framework of state-level discretion.

== THE METHOD VISIBLE IN THE LOUISIANA CASE ==

The Louisiana decision illustrates a recurring Lincoln pattern. He accepts an
imperfect outcome as a starting point. He publicly identifies the direction of further
improvement. He uses his public stance to shift the radius of acceptable subsequent
moves. He does not condition acceptance of the imperfect outcome on guarantees about
the further improvement, because doing so would foreclose the imperfect-but-operational
starting point. The pattern: act now on what is available, advocate for what should
follow, accept the gap between the two as the working condition of political
progress.

== THE PRESIDENTIAL VS. CONGRESSIONAL AUTHORITY QUESTION ==

A persistent feature of Lincoln's Reconstruction reasoning is his refusal to settle
the legal question of whether seceded states had ever truly left the Union. He treated
the question as analytically hopeless: any answer constrained policy in damaging ways.
If states had legally left, Reconstruction was the rebuilding of foreign territory; if
they had not, secession had been a nullity and the relevant authorities were the
existing state governments (which had led the rebellion). Lincoln declined to answer.
He treated the question as the kind of conceptual issue that a working framework can
afford to leave open, and proceeded with case-by-case action that did not depend on
the resolution.

== THE SHORT TIME WINDOW ==

Lincoln had forty-one days between his Second Inaugural and his death in which to
articulate Reconstruction policy. The decisions visible in those weeks — the meetings
with Confederate envoys at Hampton Roads in February (an attempt at negotiated peace),
the conversations with Sherman about Sherman's lenient terms to Joseph Johnston, the
April 11 speech, the cabinet meeting on April 14 in which Lincoln pressed for "no
hangings" of Confederate leadership — are consistent. The pattern across them is
that Lincoln treated the post-war as continuous with the war: the same decision
method (preserve optionality, prefer durable over expressive outcomes, expand the
radius gradually) applied across the transition.

== WHAT THE PLANNING REVEALS ==

Reconstruction planning is the cleanest example of Lincoln making decisions about
conditions that did not yet exist. He was reasoning about a post-war South he could
only partially anticipate, about congressional reactions he could only partially
predict, and about a policy domain whose categories had not yet stabilized. The
method he applied — start with achievable mechanisms, preserve optionality, accept
imperfect first steps as platforms for further movement, refuse to bind himself to
abstract rules when the domain is heterogeneous — is the same method visible in his
wartime decisions, applied to a different problem. The continuity of method across
problem types is the strongest evidence that he was operating from a coherent
framework rather than improvising decision-by-decision.
