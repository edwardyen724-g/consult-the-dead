Letter from Niccolo Machiavelli to Francesco Vettori, December 10, 1513

[This is Machiavelli's most famous letter, written from his exile at Sant'Andrea in Percussina, near San Casciano. It describes his daily routine and reveals his intellectual method. Translation based on multiple scholarly editions.]

CONTEXT: After the fall of the Florentine Republic in 1512 and the return of the Medici, Machiavelli was dismissed from his position as Second Chancellor and Secretary to the Ten of War. In early 1513 he was arrested, imprisoned, and tortured on suspicion of conspiracy against the Medici. Released but barred from public office, he retreated to his small farm outside Florence. This letter to his friend Francesco Vettori, the Florentine ambassador to Rome, is written during this period of forced exile.

TO FRANCESCO VETTORI, AMBASSADOR TO ROME:

Magnificent Ambassador,

"Divine favors were never late." I say this because I seem to have lost, not your favor, but rather the opportunity to communicate with you about our affairs. But now fortune has changed things, for I have received your letter which has made me supremely happy.

I shall tell you about my life here so that you may judge whether you would like to exchange it for yours.

I get up in the morning with the sun, and I go to a grove of mine that I am having cut down. I spend a couple of hours there, reviewing the work of the previous day and chatting with the woodcutters, who always have some trouble or other on their hands, either among themselves or with their neighbors.

When I leave the grove, I go to a spring, and from there to my bird-snaring area. I have a book with me, either Dante or Petrarch, or one of the lesser poets like Tibullus or Ovid. I read of their tender passions and their loves, remember my own, and lose myself in these pleasant thoughts for a while.

I then move along the road to the inn, speak with those who pass, ask for news of their villages, learn various things, and note the diverse tastes and different fancies of men. In the meantime comes the hour of dinner, where with my brood I eat what food this poor farm and tiny patrimony will provide.

Having dined, I return to the inn. There I usually find the host, a butcher, a miller, and two kiln operators. With these I sink to the level of the vulgar for the whole day, playing cards, and from these games arise a thousand disputes and countless insults with offensive words. Most of the time the fight is over a penny, and nevertheless we can be heard shouting from San Casciano. Trapped in this louse-ridden existence, I scrape the mold from my brain and release my feeling of being ill-treated by fortune. I am content to be driven along this road by her, to see if she will be ashamed of herself.

CRITICAL PASSAGE - THE EVENING TRANSFORMATION:

When evening comes, I return home and enter my study. On the threshold I take off my everyday clothes, covered with mud and dirt, and put on garments regal and courtly. Dressed appropriately, I enter the ancient courts of ancient men, where, received by them lovingly, I feed on the food that alone is mine and that I was born for. There I am not ashamed to speak with them and ask them the reason for their actions. And they, in their kindness, answer me. For four hours I feel no boredom, I forget every trouble, I do not dread poverty, death does not frighten me. I transfer myself entirely into them.

And because Dante says that there is no knowledge unless one retains what one has understood, I have noted down what I have profited from in their conversation and composed a short work, De Principatibus, in which I delve as deeply as I can into reflections on this subject, debating what a principality is, of what kinds they are, how they are acquired, how they are maintained, and why they are lost.

And if ever any whimsy of mine has pleased you, this one should not displease you; and to a prince, and especially to a new prince, it should be welcome. Therefore I am dedicating it to His Magnificence Giuliano.

I would like you to write me your opinion of this matter, whether I should present it or not, and if so, whether it would be better for me to take it myself or to send it.

I discussed with Filippo my present circumstances, and he agreed that I should prepare it and present it. If it does not go forward through Giuliano, then through the Magnificent Lorenzo.

Niccolo Machiavelli

ANALYTICAL NOTES ON THIS LETTER:

1. DECISION UNDER EXILE: Machiavelli chose to channel his forced exile into systematic intellectual production rather than despair or conspiracy. This reveals his pragmatic adaptation to adverse fortune.

2. THE DUAL LIFE: The contrast between his vulgar daytime activities and his elevated evening studies reveals his understanding of the gap between appearances and reality - a theme central to The Prince.

3. HISTORICAL METHOD IN ACTION: His description of "entering the ancient courts" and conversing with historical figures reveals his method of extracting wisdom from history through imaginative engagement, not mere academic study.

4. STRATEGIC CALCULATION: The letter itself is strategic - asking Vettori's advice about dedicating The Prince to the Medici. Machiavelli is attempting to use his intellectual work as a tool to regain political relevance.

5. FORTUNE AND ADAPTATION: His reference to fortune driving him along a road while hoping she will "be ashamed of herself" reveals his characteristic view that fortune must be actively navigated, not passively accepted.
