SOURCE NARRATIVE 04: On Floating Bodies — The Hydrostatics Treatise

On Floating Bodies (Greek: Peri ton ochoumenon) is preserved in two books, principally through the medieval Latin translation by William of Moerbeke (1269) and partly through the Archimedes Palimpsest, the tenth-century Greek manuscript that was overwritten as a prayer book and rediscovered in 1906. The work is the first surviving treatise of mathematical physics: it derives, from explicit assumptions about the structural behavior of fluids, a sequence of propositions on the equilibrium of submerged and floating bodies. Book I establishes the foundational propositions — including the principle now called Archimedes's principle, that any body wholly or partially immersed in fluid is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of fluid displaced. Book II treats the more difficult problem of the stability of floating segments of paraboloids, in which Archimedes determines the conditions under which a paraboloidal segment floats upright versus tilting onto its side as a function of its specific gravity and dimensions.

The decision-relevant pattern in the treatise is the construction of an axiomatic deductive structure for a domain that had previously been treated only empirically or as engineering practice. Hellenistic shipbuilders, surveyors, and water engineers had practical knowledge of buoyancy; no one before Archimedes had reduced the domain to an axiomatic system from which propositions could be deduced rigorously. The work's first postulate — that fluid is so constituted that "those parts which are equally compressed and continuous to one another must yield to that which is compressed more" — is a formalization of an empirical fact into a postulate from which formal proofs can proceed. Archimedes is taking a domain that had been engineering practice and reconstructing it as mathematics. The same move recurs in Equilibrium of Planes, where mechanics is given an axiomatic-deductive treatment.

A second pattern is the operational link to the Eureka episode. The principle formalized in Book I is the same principle that, as Vitruvius reports, Archimedes recognized in the bath when investigating Hiero's crown. The relationship between the particular forensic problem and the general theoretical treatise is the canonical instance of Archimedes's structural-yield bias: the occasion produces a particular solution, the general principle produces a treatise, and the treatise circulates independently of the occasion. On Floating Bodies makes no reference to the crown.

A third pattern is the increasing technical depth across the two books. Book I solves comparatively elementary cases — bodies fully submerged, bodies of uniform density floating in fluid of greater density, the basic principle of equilibrium. Book II addresses the difficult problem of stability of a paraboloidal segment, computing the conditions under which a segment of given specific gravity will float upright on its base or tilt off-axis. The geometric analysis required is at the upper limit of Hellenistic mathematical capacity; the propositions of Book II are widely regarded as among the most technically demanding work of antiquity. Archimedes does not flag the difficulty; he proceeds through the propositions in the same measured tone as Book I. The pattern is consistent across his treatises: the difficulty is absorbed into the deductive structure rather than presented as virtuosity.

A fourth pattern is the engineering relevance of the result. The stability analysis of Book II maps directly onto the structural problem of ship hull design: a hull whose shape and loading produce a stable upright equilibrium is seaworthy; a hull whose loading shifts the center of mass into instability will capsize. Archimedes had presumably worked through these structural questions during the construction of the Syracusia, the enormous ship that Hiero commissioned and Archimedes consulted on (Athenaeus and Plutarch report his involvement). On Floating Bodies is therefore not in any narrow sense a "pure" mathematical treatise; it is the formal generalization of analytical capacity that had been applied to specific engineering problems. But its publication into the Alexandrian channel as a free-standing work of mathematics preserves the channel-bifurcation: the engineering applications remain implicit, the theoretical structure is what is formally published.

A fifth pattern visible in the work is the use of indirect proof. Archimedes's propositions on stability proceed by reductio ad absurdum at multiple key steps: he assumes the contradictory of the proposition to be proven, derives a contradiction, and concludes the proposition. The technique was standard Greek mathematical practice but is deployed with unusual frequency and at high technical density in On Floating Bodies. The pattern of structural reliance on indirect proof recurs across his treatises (in Quadrature of the Parabola, in the Method of Exhaustion proofs of On the Sphere and Cylinder, in On Spirals).

A sixth pattern is the absence of empirical illustration. The treatise contains no experimental description, no apparatus diagram, no numerical example drawn from a measured case. The work is formal throughout, with the postulates serving as the only link to physical fact. The choice is consistent with Archimedes's mature publication style: the formal treatises do not display the heuristic methods by which the propositions were discovered (those methods are confessed only in The Method, sent privately to Eratosthenes near the end of his life). What is published is the rigorous deductive structure; the operational discovery process is kept separate.

On Floating Bodies is therefore the canonical instance of Archimedes's mathematization of an engineering domain, with the publication channel preserving the formal-theoretical character of the treatise independent of the engineering occasions that almost certainly informed it.
