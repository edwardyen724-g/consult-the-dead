NOTE: Much of Books 71-72 survived only through the 11th-century Byzantine epitomator Xiphilinus. The account effectively begins around 172 CE rather than Marcus's accession in 161 CE.

== BOOK 71 (EPITOME) — LUCIUS VERUS AND THE PARTHIAN WAR ==

Marcus becomes emperor and immediately makes Lucius Verus co-ruler. Marcus is described as frail and scholarly, studying under Sextus of Boeotia and Hermogenes; devoted to Stoic philosophy. Lucius is sent to fight Parthia; delegates command to general Cassius, who defeats Vologaesus, burns Seleucia, and destroys the palace at Ctesiphon. Roman bridge-building technique on the Euphrates described in detail. Lucius allegedly later plotted against Marcus and died by poison.

== BOOK 72 (EPITOME) — MARCUS AURELIUS AND THE GERMAN WARS ==

SECTIONS 1-4: GERMAN INVASIONS AND ROMAN RESPONSE
Six thousand Langobardi and Obii cross the Ister but are routed by Roman forces under Vindex and Candidus. Many other German tribes advance deep into Italy, causing widespread destruction. Marcus fights almost his entire reign against barbarians along the Ister. Marcus refuses to give soldiers extra pay after victories: the money "would be wrung from the blood of their parents and kinsmen." The Bucoli revolt in Egypt: led by a priest named Isidorus, insurgents disguised as women ambush a Roman officer, perform human sacrifice, and nearly overrun Alexandria before Cassius (the general) defeats them by sowing discord among their factions.

SECTIONS 5-10: THE RAIN MIRACLE (THUNDERING LEGION)
Roman forces fighting the Quadi are surrounded, exhausted, and dying of thirst in summer heat. "Suddenly many clouds gathered and a mighty rain burst upon them" — the Roman version held this was summoned by an Egyptian magician named Harnuphis invoking Mercury. Soldiers "turned their faces upwards and received the water in their mouths; then some held out their shields and some their helmets to catch it." Simultaneously, violent thunderbolts and hail struck the enemy. Xiphilinus's interpolation disputes Dio: he credits Christian soldiers of the "Melitene" legion who prayed, and states Marcus honored Christians by official decree and named the unit the "Thundering Legion." Dio himself had attributed the miracle to Harnuphis, not Christians.

SECTIONS 11-16: BARBARIAN DIPLOMACY
Marcus holds court in Pannonia to receive embassies from many barbarian nations. The Astingi (led by Raüs and Raptus) arrive with all their households seeking land in exchange for military service; Marcus refuses land but permits them to settle temporarily and raid the Costoboci. The Lacringes reject the Astingi; the Osi (a Pannonian people speaking a Germanic language) send envoys. Quadi receive peace and market rights, but markets are denied to Marcomani and Iazyges for fear they'll spy on Roman dispositions. Ariogaesus (a Quadic chief) resists — Marcus issues a public proclamation: anyone delivering him alive gets 1,000 gold pieces; anyone killing him gets 500. Ariogaesus is eventually captured and exiled to Alexandria.

Iazyges are defeated on land and on the frozen river Ister; remarkable episode of Roman soldiers fighting cavalry on ice.

SECTIONS 17-21: PEACE AND AFTERMATH
The revolt of Cassius in Syria forces Marcus to make peace with the Iazyges against his will. Iazyges send envoys requesting modifications to the peace terms; Marcus grants some, refuses others (particularly the request to settle close to the Ister). Naristi desert to Rome in force (3,000 men); Marcus settles various barbarian peoples within Roman territory as farmers. Three thousand Quadi allowed to settle in Roman territory.

SECTIONS 22-30: THE REVOLT OF AVIDIUS CASSIUS
Cassius, governor of Syria, declares himself emperor, reportedly encouraged by Empress Faustina, who feared what would happen to Commodus if Marcus died first. Cassius's speech to his troops is quoted at length: "Fellow-soldiers: I have come before you, not to express indignation, but to bewail my fate." He presents himself as acting for the good of the state.

Marcus responds with characteristic restraint — he addresses his troops expressing grief rather than rage, calling Cassius "my dearest friend." "There is only one thing I fear, fellow-soldiers, for you shall be told the whole truth — lest some one kill him without my orders, thinking to do me a favor." Marcus wishes to take Cassius alive so he can pardon him.

Cassius is killed by a centurion (not on Marcus's orders) after ruling as a pretender for three months and six days. Marcus refuses to look at Cassius's severed head when it is brought to him and orders it buried. Marcus refuses all barbarian auxiliary forces offered against Cassius: "it would be a terrible thing if, after conquering barbarians, we owed the victory to barbarians."

Marcus neither kills, imprisons, nor puts under guard any senator who had supported Cassius. He writes to the senate asking that no conspirator be put to death, no senator banished, no property confiscated. He reportedly burned all letters found in Cassius's possession without reading them, to avoid being tempted to punish those who wrote critically of him. Faustina dies around this time; Marcus expresses grief and requests senate honors for her.

SECTIONS 31-35: FINAL CAMPAIGNS AND CHARACTER
A law is passed: no one may govern the province from which he originally came. Marcus visits Athens, is initiated into the Mysteries, and bestows honors on the Athenians; he also establishes funded chairs for philosophy. Scythian troubles resume; Marcus arranges his son Commodus's marriage to Crispina earlier than intended. Marcus dies on March 17, 180 CE, reportedly with physicians hastening his end as a favor to Commodus. His reign: 19 years, age at death: 58 (some sources say 61). Character assessment: "he refrained from all offences and did nothing amiss whether voluntarily or involuntarily"; maintained consistent virtue from youth to death; took very little food (always at night, never during the day); spent 11-12 days on single legal cases to ensure justice; remitted all debts to the state going back 45 years.

SECTION 36: HIS EDUCATION AND THE IVORY-SHOULDERS DREAM
Before being named Caesar, Marcus dreamed he had shoulders and arms of ivory — when he tested them they proved stronger than bone. He was trained in rhetoric under Herodes Atticus and Fronto, and in philosophy under multiple teachers; "even when he was emperor he showed no shame about resorting to a teacher."
