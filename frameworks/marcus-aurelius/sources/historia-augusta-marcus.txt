Historia Augusta: Life of Marcus Antoninus (Aurelius)
Source: Penelope/University of Chicago — public domain Loeb-era translation
URL: https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Historia_Augusta/Marcus_Aurelius/

== PART 1 ==

SECTION 1: FAMILY AND BIRTH
Marcus Antoninus was "devoted to philosophy as long as he lived and pre-eminent among emperors in purity of life." His father, Annius Verus, died while serving as praetor. His grandfather, also named Annius Verus, achieved a second consulship, served as city prefect, and was enrolled among patricians by Vespasian and Titus. His mother was Domitia Lucilla, daughter of Calvisius Tullus (consul twice). Marcus was "born at Rome on the sixth day before the Kalends of May" during his grandfather's second consulship. He had a younger sister, Annia Cornificia, and married his cousin Annia Faustina. Originally named Catilius Severus after his maternal grandfather, Hadrian later called him Annius Verissimus ("most true/genuine"), then Annius Verus after his father's death.

SECTION 2: EDUCATION AND PHILOSOPHY
From childhood, Marcus was "a solemn child from the very beginning." His teachers included literature, drama, music, geometry, grammar, and oratory instructors — including the famous Herodes Atticus and Cornelius Fronto. At age twelve, Marcus "adopted the dress and the hardiness of a philosopher, pursuing his studies clad in a rough Greek cloak and sleeping on the ground." His philosophy teachers included Apollonius of Chalcedon (Stoic), Sextus of Chaeronea, and especially Junius Rusticus, whom he "ever revered" and whose disciple he became. He also studied jurisprudence with Lucius Volusius Maecianus and was "very generous" to fellow students.

SECTION 4: EARLY HONORS AND ADOPTION
Hadrian enrolled Marcus in the equestrian order at age six and appointed him to the college of the Salii at age eight. During a Salii ceremony, his crown "fell on the brow of Mars," regarded as an omen of future rule. At fifteen, he assumed the toga virilis and was betrothed to the daughter of Lucius Ceionius Commodus. He served as city prefect during the Latin Festival and conducted himself "very brilliantly" before magistrates and the emperor.

SECTION 5: IMPERIAL ADOPTION
After Lucius Caesar's death, Hadrian selected Antoninus Pius for adoption, with provisions that Pius would adopt Marcus. When Marcus learned of his adoption at eighteen, he "was appalled rather than overjoyed." He took the name Aurelius, entering "the Aurelian family, that is, into the family of Antoninus."

SECTION 6: RISE UNDER ANTONINUS PIUS
Pius broke Marcus' betrothal to Lucius Ceionius Commodus's daughter. Marcus married Faustina and had a daughter. Pius designated him as colleague in the consulship, gave him the title of Caesar, appointed him one of six commanders of the equestrian order, and gave him residence in "the House of Tiberius." Marcus was appointed consul a second time and received tribunician and proconsular power. For "three and twenty years" he maintained his father's home with such devotion that Pius "felt more affection for him day by day."

SECTION 7: SUCCESSION AND CO-RULE
When Antoninus Pius died, he "formally named him as his successor in the empire" and gave the watchword "Equanimity" to the officer of the day. Upon assuming power, Marcus made his brother Lucius his colleague, "giving him the name Lucius Aurelius Verus Commodus." He betrothed Lucius to his daughter Lucilla. The emperors promised twenty thousand sesterces to common soldiers and conducted Pius' funeral with "elaborate funeral rites."

SECTION 8: EARLY RULE AND WARS
The two emperors "acted in so democratic a manner that no one missed the lenient ways of Pius." A severe Tiber flood and famine required their "personal care and aid." The Parthian War, "which Vologaesus planned under Pius" and declared under Marcus and Verus, began after the defeat of Attidius Cornelianus. Marcus sent his brother Verus to conduct the war while remaining at Rome "where conditions demanded the presence of an emperor." Verus "lingered amid the debaucheries of Antioch and Daphne" yet received the title Imperator for victories achieved through his legates.

SECTION 9: MILITARY VICTORIES AND TITLES
Under Statius Priscus, Armenia was conquered with Artaxata taken. Both emperors received the title "Armeniacus," though Marcus "refused at first, by reason of his modesty." After the Parthian War ended, both were called "Parthicus," but Marcus again refused initially before accepting.

SECTION 10: LEGAL AND ADMINISTRATIVE REFORMS
Marcus made the senate judge in many inquiries and showed exceptional respect to the senate. He entrusted dispute settlement to many officials "in order that their prestige might be enhanced through their administration of law." He appointed a special praetor to oversee guardians and decided that youths "might have them appointed without being obliged to show cause." He added court days "until he had set 230 days for the pleading of cases."

SECTION 11: PUBLIC WELFARE MEASURES
Marcus was "exceedingly careful" in public expenditures and forbade "all libels on the part of false informers." He devised measures "for the support of the state-poor" and appointed supervisors for communities. He limited gladiatorial shows and decreed that theatrical performers should not cause expenses exceeding ten aurei. He maintained "the streets of the city and the highways with the greatest care."

SECTION 12: CHARACTER AND GOVERNANCE
Toward the people, he "acted just as one acts in a free state," being "exceedingly reasonable both in restraining men from evil and in urging them to good." He was "generous in rewarding and quick to forgive," making "bad men good, and good men very good." After Verus returned from Syria, both received the title "Father of his Country." Marcus triumphed with Lucius but later called himself only "Germanicus," the title earned in his own war.

SECTIONS 13-14: THE MARCOMANNIC WAR
The Marcomannic War broke out after the Parthian conflict. Marcus performed ceremonies and purified the city. A plague ravaged the population, with "dead removed in carts and waggons." The emperors ratified "very stringent laws on burial and tombs." The two emperors "set forth" to confront barbarian threats. Several tribes retreated, and the Quadi agreed not to confirm their new king without imperial approval. After crossing the Alps, they "completed all measures necessary for the defence of Italy and Illyricum." On the return journey, "Lucius died from a stroke of apoplexy while riding in the carriage with his brother."

== PART 2 ==

SECTION 15
Marcus read documents at circus games and was publicly ridiculed for this habit. Freedmen Geminas and Agaclytus held significant power during his reign. Despite Verus' vices offending him, Marcus "concealed and defended them; he also deified him after his death." After Verus died, rumors circulated that Marcus had poisoned him.

SECTION 16
Marcus showed great kindness to family members, bestowing office insignia on all relatives. Regarding his son Commodus, Marcus "hastened to bestow the name of Caesar, then afterward the priesthood and the title of imperator." After Verus' death, Marcus "held the empire alone, a nobler man by far and more abounding in virtues."

SECTION 17
Marcus "carried on a successful campaign against the Germans" and waged the Marcomannic war "at a time when a grievous pestilence had carried away thousands of civilians and soldiers." He "drained the treasury for this war" and held "a public sale in the Forum of the Deified Trajan" selling imperial furnishings. The "sale lasted for two months" and generated sufficient funds that buyers could return purchases for refunds.

SECTION 18
Marcus "ruled with the good-will of all" and "in the eighteenth year of his reign and the sixty-first of his life he closed his last day." At his funeral, "the senate and people, sitting together, as was never done before or after, hailed him as a gracious god." He "left one son Commodus; and had he been truly fortunate he would not have left a son."

SECTION 20
After Verus' death, Marcus "conveyed his body to Rome and laid it in the tomb of his fathers." He indicated the "strategic plans whereby the Parthians had been overcome were his own." He married his daughter to "Claudius Pompeianus, the son of a Roman knight, and now advanced in years, a native of Antioch, whose birth was not sufficiently noble."

SECTION 21
Marcus' legates succeeded "against the Mauri, when they wasted almost the whole of Spain." Against the Bucolici in Egypt, "Avidius Cassius later attempted to seize the throne." Marcus lost his "seven-year-old son, by name Verus Caesar, from an operation on a tumour under his ear. For no more than five days did he mourn him." During the pestilence, he "trained slaves for military service, whom he called Volunteers" and "armed gladiators also, calling them the Compliant."

SECTION 22
Multiple nations "banded together against us — the Marcomanni, Varistae, Hermunduri and Quadi, the Suebians, Sarmatians" and many others. Marcus "conquered these exceedingly fierce peoples, accepted the surrender of the Marcomanni, and brought a great number of them to Italy." His maxim was: "It is juster that I should yield to the counsel of such a number of such friends than that such a number of such friends should yield to my wishes."

SECTION 23
Marcus was "very sparing of the public money in giving largess" yet "furnished aid to towns on the brink of ruin, and, when necessity demanded, cancelled tribute or taxes." He "abolished common baths for both sexes" and "reformed the morals of the matrons and young nobles which were growing lax."

SECTION 24
Marcus "punished all crimes with lighter penalties than were usually inflicted by the laws." He "settled innumerable foreigners on Roman soil." He "wished to make a province of Marcomannia and likewise of Sarmatia" but "Avidius Cassius just then raised a rebellion in the East."

SECTION 25
Upon learning of Cassius' revolt, Marcus "set out against him." When Cassius was killed, "Marcus did not rejoice at Cassius' death, and gave orders that his head should be buried." Marcus "forbade the senate to impose any heavy punishment" on conspirators and "requested that during his rule no senator should be executed."

SECTION 26
Marcus "conducted many negotiations with kings, and ratified peace with all the kings and satraps of Persia." In Egypt, he "conducted himself like a private citizen and a philosopher." "In the village of Halala, in the foothills of Mount Taurus, he lost his wife Faustina, who succumbed to a sudden illness." He established "a new order of Faustinian girls in honour of his dead wife."

SECTION 27
Marcus "had himself initiated into the Eleusinian mysteries in order to prove that he was innocent of any wrong-doing." His favorite saying was from Plato: "those states prospered where the philosophers were kings or the kings philosophers."

SECTION 28: DEATH
As Marcus grew ill, he "summoned his son and besought him first of all not to think lightly of what remained of the war." Refusing food and drink, he hastened death. On his deathbed, he said to friends: "Why do you weep for me, instead of thinking about the pestilence and about death which is the common lot of us all?" When asked to whom he commended his son, he replied: "To you, if he prove worthy, and to the immortal gods." He "foresaw that after his death Commodus would turn out as he actually did, and expressed the wish that his son might die, lest he should become another Nero, Caligula, or Domitian."

SECTION 29
It was "held to Marcus' discredit that he advanced his wife's lovers, Tertullus and Tutilius and Orfitus and Moderatus, to various offices of honour." Before his final war, Marcus "swore in the Capitol that no senator had been executed with his knowledge and consent." After Faustina's death, "Fabia tried to manoeuvre a marriage with him. But he took a concubine instead rather than put a stepmother over so many children."
