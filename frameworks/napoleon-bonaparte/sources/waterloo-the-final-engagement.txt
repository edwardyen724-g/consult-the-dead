Waterloo: The Decision-Making Failures of a Single Day
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Chandler (Campaigns of Napoleon), Houssaye's
1815 reconstruction, the after-action reports of Soult, Ney, Drouot, Grouchy,
the Wellington dispatches, and Schom's account of June 18, 1815.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FRAMED IT ==

By June 16, 1815, with Ligny won against Blücher and Quatre Bras inconclusive
against Wellington, his strategic position required defeating Wellington's
Anglo-Allied army before Blücher could rejoin. The framing was straightforward:
keep Blücher out of the engagement (Grouchy's task with 33,000 men) and
destroy Wellington's smaller force at Waterloo. The window was narrow — perhaps
24 hours — but the geometry was familiar: it was the same kind of
center-of-gravity problem he had solved at Marengo, Austerlitz, and Friedland.

== THE FRAMING DECISION'S DEPENDENCIES ==

The framing assumed: (a) Grouchy could fix Blücher; (b) Wellington's army would
collapse under sustained French attack; (c) the morning rain would dry enough
by midday to permit artillery deployment; (d) his own capacity to coordinate
had not degraded since 1813. Each assumption was independently reasonable;
each was load-bearing; the campaign succeeded only if all four held. The
construct's reliability depends on whether assumption-stacking is a method
he had refined or a habit that was only safe when he had margin to absorb
one failure.

== THE PRINCIPLE THAT FAILED IN EXECUTION ==

The Grand Battery did not open fire until 11:30 AM (later than usual,
attributed to mud). The frontal assault on Hougoumont was a feint that
absorbed more reserves than intended. Ney led massed cavalry charges
without infantry or artillery support against unbroken British squares
between 4 and 6 PM, costing the cavalry reserve. The Imperial Guard's
attack at 7:30 PM was committed too late and unsupported. By that hour
Blücher's Prussians had reached the field on the French right. Each
operational failure on its own was within the range of normal Napoleonic
day-of-battle adjustment. Stacked together, they exhausted his reserve
before the decisive moment arrived.

== THE PRINCIPLE OF "WHO IS DECIDING WHAT" ==

A consistent feature of his late battles is that he delegated tactical
execution to subordinates while remaining strategic commander. This worked
when his subordinates were peers like Davout or Lannes. By 1815 several of
his best marshals were dead (Lannes), in Russian service (Bernadotte),
absent (Davout was Minister of War in Paris), or impaired (Berthier had
died in suspicious circumstances on June 1). The man on the field directing
tactical execution was Ney, who was brave but had a record of impulsive
decisions. He let Ney run the cavalry charges because the system he had
built required someone to run them and Ney was who was available. The
construct of strategic-bandwidth-via-trusted-subordinates broke when the
trusted subordinates were no longer present.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he wrote multiple accounts of Waterloo, each blaming
different subordinates: Grouchy for not fixing Blücher; Ney for the
unsupported cavalry charges; Soult for staff incompetence after Berthier;
the rain. Each blame is partially correct. The structural fact he did
not fully address: the campaign required his full powers AND his full
team simultaneously, and by 1815 he had only one of the two. He chose
to fight anyway because the political alternative (slow degradation of
the Hundred Days regime by foreign pressure) was certain defeat, while
Waterloo offered a chance.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

Waterloo shows the construct's late-career failure mode: methods optimized
for a specific organizational support system fail when the support system
has decayed. He did not adapt his decision-making style to the depleted
team; he ran 1815 the way he had run 1805. The construct is not
transferable to a degraded version of itself — it requires the full
infrastructure of high-quality marshals, reliable staff coordination, and
trusted intelligence, and below a threshold of any of these, the entire
construct underperforms. The decision to fight Waterloo was sound given
the Hundred Days position; the decision to fight Waterloo with the team
he had was not separately considered.
