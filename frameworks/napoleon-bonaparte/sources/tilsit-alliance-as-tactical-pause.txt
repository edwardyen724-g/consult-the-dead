Tilsit: The Theatrical Alliance and the Logic of Provisional Friendship
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Caulaincourt's account of the Tilsit meeting
(June 25–July 9, 1807), the secret articles of the Treaty of Tilsit, the
Correspondance with Talleyrand and Champagny, and Tulard's analysis of the
diplomatic strategy.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FRAMED IT ==

After Friedland (June 14, 1807) the Russian army was beaten but not destroyed.
A continued advance into Russia was logistically marginal in summer and impossible
in winter. The political prize on offer was greater than the military prize remaining:
a Russian alliance against Britain would close the eastern half of Europe to British
trade and complete the Continental System. The decision was whether to settle for
a battlefield-equivalent peace or to design a more ambitious arrangement that
turned a defeated enemy into a temporary partner.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

He arranged the famous raft meeting on the Niemen — staged so that Tsar Alexander
crossed onto neutral water to meet him. The choreography mattered: it placed the
two emperors on apparent equality, which protected Alexander's domestic legitimacy
(he could not have visited a victor's court). He spent eight days in personal
proximity with Alexander, flattering him, inviting him into a shared imperial
imagination, framing Britain as their common enemy and Europe as their joint
project. The relationship he was building was real-feeling and instrumental at
once.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE PERFORMANCE ==

He understood that an alliance with a defeated power requires the defeated power's
ruler to feel he has gained, not lost. The performance of equality at Tilsit gave
Alexander a domestic story he could carry home: I was not conquered; I joined the
greatest power in Europe in a partnership of equals. The structural move was to
manufacture a face-saving narrative for Alexander that converted Russian humiliation
into Russian participation. The treaty terms were generous on territory (Russia
kept its core; Prussia paid the price) and harsh on policy (closed ports). He
purchased policy with territory because territory gave Alexander something to
display while policy was something he could later renege on quietly.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S BUILT-IN EXPIRATION ==

The construct also contained its own expiration date. Alexander returned to St.
Petersburg, faced an aristocracy whose income depended on British grain trade,
and found the Continental System untenable. By 1810 Russia was openly violating
the treaty's economic clauses. He had built an alliance whose performative core
(Tsar treated as equal) was inverted from its operational core (Tsar must
implement his policy). The two cores could coexist for only as long as the
performance suppressed the operational reality. When the operational reality
reasserted itself, the alliance broke.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he described Tilsit as the high-water mark of his system and
the moment after which everything was either consolidation or decline. He
attributed the breakdown to Alexander's unreliability. Caulaincourt's contemporary
analysis is more accurate: the alliance was structured to extract from Alexander
behavior contrary to Russia's economic interests, and no amount of imperial
flattery could overcome the domestic pressure once it accumulated.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

Tilsit exhibits his characteristic mode of converting force outcomes into
political instruments through staged personal diplomacy. The decision rule is:
when you have battlefield leverage, do not extract maximum territorial gain;
extract maximum policy commitment, paid for with territorial generosity. The
rule works whenever the policy commitment can survive once the personal
relationship cools. It fails when the policy is structurally incompatible with
the partner's domestic interests, regardless of what the partner promised at
the table.
