The Grande Armée: Logistics as the Architecture of Speed
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Colin (L'éducation militaire), Chandler's analysis
of the corps system, the orders for the 1805 Ulm campaign, the Correspondance with
Berthier and Daru, and the official organizational manuals of the army.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE INHERITED IT ==

Eighteenth-century European armies moved at roughly 10 miles per day, tied to
their supply trains, deployed for battle in a single mass that required several
days to assemble or unwind. The constraint was not soldiers' legs but the column
of wagons feeding them. He inherited from the late Republic a quartermaster
system that already used local requisition more aggressively than the Bourbon
army had, but the strategic implications had not been worked out.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

He restructured the army into corps — each one a 20,000–30,000 man combined-arms
formation with its own infantry, cavalry, artillery, engineers, and staff —
capable of fighting independently for one day against larger forces. The corps
moved on parallel roads, requisitioned locally, and converged only at the moment
of battle. The framing was that LOGISTICS DETERMINES STRATEGIC OPTIONS: if you
move fast enough, you arrive before the opponent can concentrate; if the corps
is self-sufficient for 24 hours, you don't need the road network to support a
single mass; if multiple corps converge from different directions, the opponent
cannot anticipate the point of decision until it is too late to redeploy.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE STRUCTURE ==

The structural insight was that an army's speed equals the slowest necessary
linkage. Old armies were slow because every unit waited for the supply train.
The corps system distributed the supply problem so that the army's speed was
the speed of independent self-sustaining columns. Speed compounded into
strategic surprise: the 1805 march from the Channel to the Danube (over 400
miles in five weeks) arrived before Mack at Ulm understood he was the target.
The march was not faster than physics permitted; it was faster than the
opponent's planning model allowed for.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S DEPENDENCE ==

Local requisition worked in fertile densely-populated Europe — the Rhineland,
northern Italy, the German heartland. It failed in Spain (where the population
fought back and burned supplies) and catastrophically in Russia (where the
distances exceeded the corps' self-sustainment window and the country had been
scorched). The corps system was not a universal solution; it was a method
calibrated to a specific terrain and demographic profile. When the assumed
context broke, the system broke with it. Russia in 1812 saw corps starve
because they could not requisition where there were no farms.

== THE STAFF MACHINE ==

Berthier as Major-General translated his rapid verbal orders into written
movement orders for each corps within hours. Daru as commissary organized the
contracts, depots, and supply dumps. The decision-making structure was: he
generated strategic intent, Berthier produced operational orders, corps
commanders executed without waiting for further direction. The system made
his cognitive bandwidth the army's speed limit. When Berthier was absent or
exhausted (as in the 1813 campaign), the system slowed.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he treated the corps system as one of his most important
contributions, more durable than any single battle. Subsequent European armies
adopted the structure within a generation. The construct survived him because
it was a structural innovation in how forces relate to terrain and supply,
not a personal tactical genius that died with him.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

The Grande Armée's logistics show his pattern of solving battlefield problems
by restructuring the layer beneath the battlefield. Don't win battles faster;
arrive faster, so the battle is on your chosen ground at your chosen moment.
The construct is: change the inputs to the problem before the problem requires
solving. The failure mode appears when the inputs change in ways the
restructured system cannot accommodate — Russia's emptiness defeated the
system as effectively as Austrian density had enabled it.
