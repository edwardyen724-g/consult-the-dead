The Hundred Days: A Calculated Return Against Compounding Odds
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Schom (One Hundred Days), Caulaincourt's account
of the Elba period, the diaries of officers who joined him at Grenoble and Lyon,
the reports of the Vienna Congress, and Chandler's analysis of the Waterloo campaign.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FRAMED IT ==

By February 1815 his position on Elba was financially deteriorating: the Bourbon
government had not paid the pension agreed at Fontainebleau, his family was being
denied transit, and intelligence from France described the Restoration as making
itself unpopular faster than he had estimated. The Vienna Congress was approaching
agreement on terms that would make further intervention impossible. Two clocks were
running: his own resources, and the European settlement. If he moved now, both
were favorable. If he waited six months, both would be hostile.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

He sailed from Elba on February 26 with around 1,000 men. The decision was
explicitly probabilistic. He told Drouot it was a wager on French popular sentiment;
if the army fired on him at the first encounter (the Fifth Regiment at Laffrey,
March 7), the project ended and he would die or be captured. If the soldiers
crossed over, the cascade would carry him to Paris without a battle. The framing
was that the bet had to be made before the Congress concluded, and that the cost
of failure was a death he would have died anyway in a few years on a forgotten
island. The cost of NOT making the bet was certainty.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE WAGER ==

The structural insight: when the status quo is degrading and the alternative is a
binary outcome (regain the throne or die quickly), the rational move is to
accelerate the binary. Slow loss is worse than fast resolution because slow loss
denies optionality permanently while fast resolution either restores everything
or ends cleanly. He was not optimizing for survival; he was optimizing for the
chance of meaningful return given that survival on Elba was already a kind of
death.

== THE EXECUTION (FEBRUARY 26 – MARCH 20) ==

Twenty days from Elba to the Tuileries without firing a shot. The dynamic he had
predicted played out exactly: each unit sent to arrest him crossed over instead.
Marshal Ney, who had promised Louis XVIII to bring him "in an iron cage," joined
him at Auxerre. The Bourbons fled. The decision-making method was not a single
choice but a sequence of conditional commitments: each successful crossing made
the next regiment more likely to cross, and each crossing increased the political
cost to the Bourbons of fighting at all. He had structured a self-amplifying
collapse of the opposing legitimacy.

== THE LIMIT OF THE WAGER ==

What he could not amplify was the European response. The Allied powers declared
him an outlaw on March 13 and mobilized 700,000 men. The political cascade in
France did not have a counterpart in Europe. Waterloo (June 18) ended the
restoration in 100 days because the cascade he had engineered domestically had
no leverage on the coalition's decision to crush him before he could rebuild.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he treated the Hundred Days as a bet whose expected value was
positive even though the realized value was negative. The framework underneath
the bet — "an opponent's legitimacy can be made to collapse before reaching him
with force" — had worked as predicted within France. It failed when applied to
a coalition whose legitimacy did not depend on French popular sentiment. The
mistake was not the wager itself but the assumption that the same cascade
mechanism would scale internationally.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

The Hundred Days exhibits the construct of converting a slow degrading position
into a fast binary one when degradation is certain. It also shows a recurring
overreach: domestic mechanisms that worked through legitimacy cascades did not
generalize to international situations where multiple sovereign decision-makers
could not be cascade-collapsed. The decision to return was sound on its own
terms; the underlying theory of how France connects to Europe was not.
