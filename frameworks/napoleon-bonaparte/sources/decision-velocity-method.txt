Decision Velocity: The Method of Working Through Cabinet, Couriers, and Cabinet d'État
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from the Correspondance (32 volumes), Bourrienne's
account of his daily working method as First Consul, Méneval's memoirs as private
secretary (1802–1813), Roederer's notes on the Conseil d'État sessions, and
Chaptal's account of working with him on industrial policy.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FRAMED IT ==

A modern state generates more decisions per day than any single mind can
attend to with full deliberation. Most rulers solved this by delegating
decisions to ministers and reviewing only major issues. He chose the inverse:
make himself the decision bottleneck for an unprecedented number of issues,
but operate at a speed that prevented the bottleneck from becoming a
constraint. The construct on display: I will make every important decision,
and I will make it in minutes rather than days, so that velocity replaces
delegation as the system's scaling property.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

His daily working pattern: 16-hour days; documents read aloud by secretaries
while dressing or eating; dictated responses to multiple secretaries
simultaneously, switching between subjects mid-sentence; couriers sent every
few hours from camp or palace; a Conseil d'État of legal experts kept on
retainer for substantive review of major drafts. He could dictate orders for
seven different theaters of operation in a single morning. Roederer
described him as "a brain that thinks aloud at high speed in multiple
documents at once."

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE VELOCITY ==

The structural insight: in competitive environments where opponents make
decisions in days, an opponent making decisions in hours is not just faster
but operates in a different regime entirely — he can revise his decisions
in response to events before the opponent's first decision has executed.
The corps system relied on this: orders sent at 6 AM produced movement by
noon, while opponents were still in council. The same principle applied
to civil administration: a regulation drafted Monday could be issued
Wednesday and revised Friday based on prefect feedback. He compressed
the OODA loop of the State.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S COGNITIVE COST ==

Méneval describes him sleeping four hours in three sessions, working
through dictations while walking, and frequently confusing details across
parallel files. The velocity required holding too much in working memory
to be reliable across a long career. By 1812 his health was visibly
deteriorating; by 1814 his decisions in the German campaign showed
slower execution than in 1805. The construct depends on physical capacity
that ages out. There is no provision in the method for transferring the
bottleneck function to a successor; he never trained one.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S DEPENDENT ASSUMPTION ==

Working at high speed means working with the information that arrives in
real time. Information that takes weeks to reach him (Spain via the
Pyrenees, Russia in winter, Saint Helena from anywhere) became progressively
useless because his decisions had already been made on stale data. The
method assumes a Europe of telegraph-or-better speed; below that bandwidth,
his apparent decision-quality degrades because his decisions are correct
for the world of three weeks ago, not the world of today.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena, with no decisions to make and infinite time, he produced
the long, careful Mémorial that he could not have produced as Emperor. The
contrast suggests the velocity method was not a personal preference but a
strategic posture matched to the position. Without the velocity, the
bottleneck-as-strategy collapses; with it, no successor could have replaced
him without redesigning the State around themselves at a similar pace.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

His working pattern shows the construct: in environments where speed of
revision matters more than depth of any one decision, design the system so
the highest-leverage decisions can be made and revised faster than any
competitor can respond. The construct succeeds when the decision-maker has
the cognitive capacity, when information arrives at compatible speed, and
when no successor needs to inherit the system. It fails predictably along
each of these axes — and Spain, Russia, and the late Empire show all three
failures arriving in sequence.
