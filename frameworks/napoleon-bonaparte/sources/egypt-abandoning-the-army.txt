Egypt 1799: The Decision to Abandon an Army for a Political Window
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Bourrienne's memoirs, the Correspondance for
August 1799, the dispatches received in Cairo from France, and McLynn's
reconstruction of the calculations behind the August 23 departure.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FACED IT ==

By summer 1799 the Egyptian campaign had failed strategically. Nelson had destroyed
the French fleet at the Nile (August 1798); the Mediterranean was British; reinforcement
was impossible; the Syrian campaign had ended at Acre in May; plague was killing
his troops. France itself had reverses on every front (the Second Coalition's
campaigns of 1799 had cost the Republic Italy and threatened the Rhine). Letters
from his brothers and from Joseph Fouché reached him in late July describing the
Directory as discredited and a power vacuum forming. Two problems competed for his
decision: stay with the army he had led into Egypt, or abandon it for the political
opening in Paris.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

He told no one in advance except a small circle (Berthier, Lannes, Murat, Eugène).
He left Kléber in command via written orders Kléber received only after his ship had
sailed. He took a frigate squadron through the British blockade with a small staff
and considerable Egyptian loot. The framing was not abandonment but reassignment of
his own asset to the highest-return battlefield, treating himself — his name, his
reputation, his political momentum — as a transferable resource that the army no
longer needed but France did.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE CHOICE ==

The construct on display: he separated the survival of his current command from
the survival of his strategic position. The army in Egypt could be lost (and was
— Kléber was assassinated, Menou surrendered to the British in 1801) without his
trajectory being lost. Conversely, missing the political window in Paris would be
unrecoverable. He calculated which loss was reversible. A failed campaign produced
losers who could rehabilitate. A missed succession moment produced a winner who
would not need him.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S MORAL COST ==

Kléber's letters back to the Directory called the abandonment a betrayal. Officers
who learned of the departure described the army as "sold." He never publicly
addressed the criticism. The pattern is consistent with his treatment of subordinates
throughout his career: their loyalty was a tool he used at the moment of need, but
he did not consider himself bound to reciprocity when reciprocity would cost him
strategically. Loyalty for him ran one direction — toward the State and toward
his own role in shaping it — not as a peer-to-peer obligation.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he defended the departure as duty to France. The defense is
unconvincing as moral argument but accurate as strategic accounting: had he stayed,
18 Brumaire happens without him, the Consulate forms around someone else, and the
trajectory that produced the Code, the consolidation of the Revolution's gains, and
the European reordering takes a different (and likely shorter) shape. The strategic
return on departure was very high; the moral cost was real but did not register
on the calculus he was running.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

Egypt shows him treating his own presence as a deployable asset that could be
withdrawn from a failing position and reinvested in a higher-return one. Most
commanders identify with their command. He identified with his trajectory. The
operating principle: do not let sunk-cost loyalty to a current position prevent
redeployment of yourself when the highest-leverage situation is elsewhere. The
army was a tool. Tools can be left behind.
