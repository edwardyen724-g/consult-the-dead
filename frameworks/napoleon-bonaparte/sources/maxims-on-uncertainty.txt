The Military Maxims: Codified Heuristics for Decision Under Uncertainty
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from the Maximes de guerre (compiled c.1827 from
his marginalia and dictations), the Précis des guerres de César (Saint Helena),
Notes sur l'art de la guerre, and Chandler's analysis of his explicit decision
heuristics.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FRAMED IT ==

The campaigns of his early career (Italy 1796–97, Egypt 1798–99) were won by
intuition that he could not initially articulate. By the Saint Helena exile he
had ample time to reverse-engineer his own method into transmissible heuristics.
The Maxims are the result: 78 numbered propositions about how to decide under
the conditions of war. They are not descriptive — many of them prescribe behavior
he himself failed to follow at Borodino or Waterloo — but they are the cleanest
articulation of his operating method by the only person who could write it.

== THE CORE FRAMING ==

Several maxims cluster around a single insight: probability dominates over
optimization. Maxim 9: "The strength of an army, like the power in mechanics,
is estimated by multiplying the mass by the rapidity; a rapid march augments
the morale of an army." Maxim 16: "It is an approved maxim in war never to do
what the enemy wishes you to do, for this reason alone, that he wishes it."
Maxim 95: "Wars are won by him who makes the fewest mistakes, not the most
brilliant moves." The pattern: optimize against the modal opponent decision,
not against the most likely outcome of your own brilliance.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE MAXIMS ==

The decision rule embedded across the Maxims is asymmetric: assume your
opponent is intelligent, design your action so its expected value is high
across the range of intelligent counter-moves, and then execute with speed
that prevents the opponent from selecting his best counter. The Maxims do
not promise victory; they promise a positive expected value over many
encounters. The implicit assumption is that war is repeated — that a
career is composed of dozens of campaigns, and the right strategy is the
one that wins on average.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S CHARACTERISTIC FAILURE MODE ==

The Maxims assume war is repeated, but a campaign that destroys the army
ends the career. Russia 1812 violated his own Maxim 6 ("at the commencement
of a campaign, to advance or not to advance is a matter for grave
consideration") because the question "should we advance past Smolensk" was
treated as routine when its expected value was actually catastrophic. He
codified the rules but did not always follow them. The codification served
a separate purpose: instruction for successors and historical record. The
operating Napoleon and the writing Napoleon had different audiences and
different incentives.

== THE MAXIMS ON LUCK ==

Maxim 95 (preferring fewer mistakes to brilliance) and Maxim 73 ("ask of me
anything but time") together encode his attitude toward chance. He treated
fortune as a real factor that nonetheless rewarded preparation: a general
who positioned correctly would have more "luck" because more outcomes would
favor him. He explicitly preferred generals who were lucky over generals
who were merely competent — not from superstition, but from the empirical
observation that competence-without-luck failed enough times to disqualify.
The Maxims encode a subtle theory: luck is partly observable from track
record, and a track record of luck is itself selectable evidence of an
unobserved decision quality.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

The Maxims are themselves a form of post-decision reasoning. They are what
he chose to leave behind. The decisions they describe are the ones he wanted
to be remembered as having followed, not necessarily the ones he made. As
documentary evidence of his explicit heuristics they are valuable; as
descriptive accounts of how he actually decided in the moment they are
selectively edited. The two layers must be read against each other to
recover the operating method.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

The Maxims show his theory of his own theory: an attempt to make his
intuition transmissible. The construct: heuristics for asymmetric uncertainty
where the goal is to skew expected value rather than achieve a specific
outcome. The failure mode: heuristics drift from practice as the
practitioner's career extends, and the writing-Napoleon produced rules that
the operating-Napoleon did not always follow when the operating-Napoleon
was under maximum pressure.
