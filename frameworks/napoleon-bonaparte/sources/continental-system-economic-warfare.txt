The Continental System: A Decision to Wage Economic War as a Substitute for Victory
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from the Berlin Decree (November 21, 1806), the Milan
Decrees (1807), the Correspondance, Tulard's analysis, and Eli Heckscher's economic
study of the system.

== THE PROBLEM AFTER TRAFALGAR ==

After Trafalgar (October 21, 1805) the option of invading Britain was permanently
closed; the Royal Navy could not be defeated at sea within any planning horizon.
The strategic question shifted: how do you defeat an enemy you cannot reach? His
answer was to redefine the battlefield. If Britain could not be invaded, perhaps it
could be financially ruined. The Continental System was a decision to convert all of
continental Europe into a single economic instrument aimed at one target.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

The Berlin Decree closed continental ports to British shipping. The Milan Decree
extended the rule: any neutral ship that had touched a British port was treated as
British. The framing was that Britain's industrial economy depended on European
markets, and that a sustained boycott would collapse credit in London, force political
realignment, and produce the surrender he could not produce by arms. The mechanism
required not partial cooperation but absolute compliance from every continental
state — including reluctant allies like Russia and unwilling vassals like Portugal.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND THE GAMBLE ==

He treated the economic system the way he treated armies: as a machine whose output
depends on the integrity of every component. A leak in Lisbon was equivalent to an
exposed flank in battle. The decision logic chained: enforcing the system required
controlling Spain (1808), invading Portugal, pressuring Russia (Tilsit's secret
clauses), and ultimately punishing Russia when she defected (1812). Each downstream
campaign was not a separate war; each was the price of maintaining the original
economic position.

== THE PRINCIPLE'S HIDDEN COST ==

What he failed to account for was that the system imposed greater costs on his
own coalition than on Britain. French and continental industries lost colonial inputs
and overseas markets; smuggling proliferated; allied states resented the burden;
British industry adapted by finding markets in South America and the United States.
The system worked as a one-shot weapon — a sudden shock might have produced its
intended effect — but as a sustained policy it cannibalized his own base. The
construct rewards bold structural moves but punishes the moves whose costs accumulate
on you faster than on the opponent.

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he defended the Continental System as conceptually sound but
defectively executed, blaming smugglers and unreliable allies. The more accurate
reading is that he treated economic interdependence as a weapon without recognizing
that he was at one end of the same weapon. The decision pattern shows his
characteristic move — replace an unwinnable engagement with a redefined problem
he could win — and also its characteristic failure mode: assuming the redefined
problem will cooperate with his timeline.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

The Continental System illustrates his instinct to escape unwinnable contests by
shifting the dimension of competition. When direct force fails, manufacture a
different kind of force. But it also illustrates the limit case: structural moves
that depend on enforcement against the will of nominal allies become engines that
consume his own capital faster than the enemy's, and the longer they run, the more
campaigns they require to sustain themselves.
