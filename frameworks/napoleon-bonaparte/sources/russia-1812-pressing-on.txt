Russia 1812: The Decision to Press On Past Smolensk and Past Moscow
SYNTHESIZED CONTEXT — drawn from Ségur (Histoire de Napoléon et de la Grande Armée),
the Correspondance during June–December 1812, Caulaincourt's memoirs, Bourgogne's
diary, and Chandler's reconstruction of the campaign.

== THE PROBLEM AS HE FRAMED IT ==

When the Grande Armée crossed the Niemen on June 24, 1812, his stated objective
was a decisive border battle that would force Tsar Alexander to renew the Tilsit
alliance and re-enter the Continental System. The Russian armies under Barclay and
Bagration refused to give that battle. Instead they retreated, scorching the country.
By Smolensk in mid-August, the original framing had failed: there was no decisive
battle to be won at the border because the border had been abandoned. Every officer
of standing — Berthier, Murat, Caulaincourt — counseled stopping at Smolensk and
wintering on a defensible line. He chose to advance.

== THE FRAMING DECISION ==

He restated the problem. If the border battle could not be had, then Moscow itself
became the center of gravity. Moscow's capture, he reasoned, would force Alexander
to negotiate because no Tsar could survive the political shock of losing the old
capital. The original goal (force a battle) was replaced by a substitute goal (force
a peace by capturing the symbol). The substitute was kept attached to the original
language so that withdrawing felt like abandoning a working campaign rather than
admitting that the working campaign had ended.

== THE PRINCIPLE BEHIND PRESSING ON ==

The deeper pattern: when reality refuses to deliver the decisive event he has
designed for, he redesigns the event around what reality is offering, and convinces
himself the redesigned event has the same political weight. The Borodino battle
(September 7) was tactically inconclusive but cost 30,000+ French casualties he
could not replace. Moscow burned a week after he occupied it. Alexander did not
negotiate. Each substitute goal failed to produce the political effect of the
original, but he had no construct in his operating method for reversing course
once a campaign had begun. Retreating without a peace offer was, to him, defeat
made visible — and visibility was the asset he could not surrender.

== THE OCTOBER DECISION TO STAY IN MOSCOW ==

He remained in Moscow five weeks (September 14–October 19) waiting for Alexander
to send envoys. Caulaincourt urged immediate withdrawal while weather permitted.
He responded with letters to Alexander that received no reply. The decision to
stay was not optimism about negotiation; it was an inability to convert the
absent peace into a different operational plan. The frame "I am here to compel
peace" had no exit clause for "peace will not come."

== POST-DECISION REASONING ==

On Saint Helena he attributed the disaster to the early winter, which arrived
on a normal schedule. Caulaincourt's contemporary letters and Ségur's reconstruction
show the army was destroyed not by November snow but by lack of forage, lack of
discipline once retreat began, and the absence of a planned line of withdrawal.
The campaign died because the original operational logic — fight a decisive
battle — had been silently replaced by a political wager on the Tsar's psychology,
and the wager had no contingency plan.

== WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT THE OPERATING METHOD ==

Russia exposes the failure mode of his strongest construct: the willingness to
redesign the problem mid-campaign. The same flexibility that produced Austerlitz
(redesign the battle around what the enemy will do) destroyed the army when applied
to a campaign whose enemy refused to do anything. Bold reframing requires the
opponent's participation; against a passive opponent who simply withdraws, the
reframing collapses into wishful continuation.
